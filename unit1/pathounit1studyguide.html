Chapter 1: Introduction to Pathophysiology

Etiology - Causes of Disease

▼
Etiology
The study of the causes of disease.
🦠 Pathogen-Caused
Diseases caused by microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites). Examples: pneumonia, influenza.
🏥 Nosocomial
Hospital-acquired infections NOT present at admission. Examples: MRSA, C. diff, catheter-associated UTIs.
⚕️ Iatrogenic
Caused by medical examination or treatment. Examples: medication side effects, surgical complications.
❓ Idiopathic
Disease with unknown cause. Examples: idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis.

Levels of Prevention

▼
🛡️ Primary Prevention
Protecting people from developing disease. BEFORE disease occurs. Examples: Vaccinations, health education.
🔍 Secondary Prevention
Early detection through screening. WHEN disease is early. Examples: Mammograms, colonoscopies.
🏥 Tertiary Prevention
Rehabilitation after diagnosis. AFTER disease established. Examples: Cardiac rehab, physical therapy.

Cellular Adaptations

▼

Figure: Common Cellular Adaptations

📉 Atrophy
Decrease in cell size. Causes: Disuse, denervation, ischemia. Example: Muscle atrophy from immobilization.
📈 Hypertrophy
Increase in cell size. Cells get BIGGER. Example: Cardiac hypertrophy from hypertension.
🔢 Hyperplasia
Increase in cell NUMBER. Cells multiply. Example: Liver regeneration.
🔄 Metaplasia
Replacement of one cell type by another. Example: Smoker's bronchial epithelium changes.
⚠️ Dysplasia
Abnormal cell changes - pre-neoplastic. Example: Cervical dysplasia.
🚨 Neoplasia
Uncontrolled cell growth. May be benign or malignant (cancer).

Apoptosis vs Necrosis

▼
🧬 Apoptosis
Programmed cell death - normal, controlled, NO inflammation. Example: Fetal hand development.
💀 Necrosis
Unplanned cell death from injury, causes inflammation. Example: Myocardial infarction.

Chapter 4: Pain

Pain Fundamentals

▼
🔬 Nociceptors
Specialized nerve endings that detect painful stimuli.
🔥 Acute Pain
Sudden onset, identifiable cause, resolves with healing. < 3-6 months.
😰 Chronic Pain
Persists beyond normal healing time. > 3-6 months.
🚪 Gate Control Theory
A "gate" in the spinal cord modulates pain signals. Distraction "closes" the gate.
📍 Referred Pain
Pain felt at location different from source. Example: MI pain in left arm.

Compensated vs Uncompensated

✅ Fully Compensated

pH Normal (7.35-7.45)

Body successfully compensated. Both systems show abnormal values but pH is normal.

Example:
pH: 7.38 ✓
CO₂: 48 (high)
HCO₃: 28 (high - compensation)
⚠️ Partially Compensated

pH Abnormal but moving toward normal

Body is compensating, but pH hasn't normalized yet.

Example:
pH: 7.32 (low)
CO₂: 52 (high - primary)
HCO₃: 26 (high - starting compensation)
🚨 Uncompensated

pH Abnormal with no compensation

Most dangerous state. Acute or compensation failed.

Example:
pH: 7.25 (low)
CO₂: 60 (high - primary)
HCO₃: 24 (normal - no compensation)

🏥 Case Study 1: Post-Operative Patient

Scenario

Mrs. Johnson, a 72-year-old female, is post-operative day 2 following abdominal surgery. She is reluctant to deep breathe due to incisional pain. Her respiratory rate has been 10-12/min for the past 6 hours. She appears drowsy and confused.

Vital Signs & Labs

HR98 bpm
BP138/88 mmHg
RR10/min
SpO291% on RA
pH7.28
PaCO258 mmHg
HCO326 mEq/L
K+4.0 mEq/L

🧠 Clinical Thinking Process

Step 1: Analyze pH

pH is 7.28 - this is low (acidosis). Normal is 7.35-7.45.

Step 2: Identify Primary Cause

CO₂ is 58 (high) and HCO3 is 26 (normal). The elevated CO₂ is causing the acidosis. This points to a respiratory problem.

Step 3: Assess Compensation

HCO3 is still normal (26 mEq/L), meaning the kidneys haven't had time to compensate yet. This is uncompensated.

Step 4: Identify the Problem

Shallow breathing (10/min) → CO₂ retention → Respiratory acidosis. Cause: Incisional pain preventing deep breathing.

✅ Diagnosis & Interventions

ABG Interpretation: Uncompensated Respiratory Acidosis

Priority Nursing Actions:

  • Encourage deep breathing and coughing exercises (incentive spirometry)
  • Adequate pain control to allow for deep breathing
  • Position patient in semi-Fowler's or high Fowler's
  • Monitor respiratory status and ABGs
  • Notify provider if condition worsens - may need respiratory support

Why this matters: Post-operative patients are at high risk for respiratory complications. Early recognition and intervention prevents the need for intubation.

💧 Case Study 2: The Diuretic Dilemma

Scenario

Mr. Thompson, a 68-year-old male with heart failure, has been taking furosemide (Lasix) 80mg daily for 2 weeks. He presents with weakness, muscle cramps, and palpitations. He mentions he's been "peeing a lot" and feels dizzy when standing up quickly.

Vital Signs & Labs

HR112 bpm, irregular
BP98/62 mmHg
Na+138 mEq/L
K+2.8 mEq/L
Ca2+9.2 mg/dL
Mg2+1.2 mEq/L
ECGFlattened T waves, U waves

🧠 Clinical Thinking Process

Step 1: Identify the Medication Effect

Furosemide (Lasix) is a loop diuretic that causes excretion of Na+, K+, and water. Potassium loss is a major side effect.

Step 2: Analyze the Potassium Level

K+ is 2.8 mEq/L - this is severe hypokalemia (normal: 3.5-5.0). This explains the muscle cramps, weakness, and cardiac effects.

Step 3: Connect ECG Changes

Flattened T waves and U waves are classic ECG signs of hypokalemia. The irregular heart rate indicates cardiac involvement - this is dangerous!

Step 4: Identify Additional Issues

Mg2+ is also low (1.2 mEq/L). Hypomagnesemia makes hypokalemia harder to correct - both need to be addressed. Orthostatic hypotension suggests volume depletion.

✅ Diagnosis & Interventions

Primary Problem: Severe Hypokalemia with Hypomagnesemia (diuretic-induced)

Priority Nursing Actions:

  • Cardiac monitoring - risk of arrhythmias is high!
  • IV potassium replacement (with provider order)
  • IV magnesium replacement (must correct Mg to correct K)
  • Oral potassium supplements once stable
  • Education: K+-rich foods (bananas, oranges, potatoes)
  • May need K+-sparing diuretic added to regimen

Key Point: Never give IV potassium push! Always diluted and via infusion pump. Monitor I&O and daily weights.

🩺 Case Study 3: The Mysterious Weakness

Scenario

A 55-year-old female presents to the ED with progressive weakness, tingling around her mouth, and muscle spasms in her hands. She recently had a thyroidectomy 3 weeks ago. She states, "I just don't feel right, and my hands keep cramping up."

Vital Signs & Labs

HR62 bpm
BP128/76 mmHg
Temp98.4°F
Ca2+6.8 mg/dL
K+4.2 mEq/L
Na+140 mEq/L
Phosphate5.2 mg/dL

🧠 Clinical Thinking Process

Step 1: Consider the History

Recent thyroidectomy - this is key! The parathyroid glands are located behind the thyroid and can be damaged or removed during surgery.

Step 2: Analyze the Calcium

Ca2+ is 6.8 mg/dL - severe hypocalcemia (normal: 8.5-10.5). This explains ALL symptoms: perioral tingling, muscle spasms, weakness.

Step 3: Connect the Signs

Perioral tingling + hand spasms = classic signs of hypocalcemia. Positive Chvostek's (facial twitch) and Trousseau's (carpal spasm) signs would likely be present.

Step 4: Understand the Mechanism

Parathyroid hormone (PTH) regulates calcium. Damage to parathyroids → ↓ PTH → ↓ calcium. High phosphate is also consistent (PTH normally excretes phosphate).

✅ Diagnosis & Interventions

Diagnosis: Acute Hypocalcemia secondary to Hypoparathyroidism (post-thyroidectomy)

Priority Nursing Actions:

  • IV calcium gluconate - for severe symptomatic hypocalcemia
  • Cardiac monitoring - hypocalcemia can cause arrhythmias
  • Seizure precautions
  • Keep IV access patent - calcium can cause tissue damage if extravasated
  • Patient education: lifelong calcium and vitamin D supplements likely needed
  • Monitor for laryngospasm - emergency airway equipment nearby

Key Teaching Point: Post-thyroidectomy patients need monitoring for hypocalcemia. Symptoms can appear days to weeks after surgery. Early recognition prevents life-threatening complications!
