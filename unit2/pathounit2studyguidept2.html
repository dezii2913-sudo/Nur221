🛡️ Chapter 11: Lymphatic System

Lymphatic System Overview

The lymphatic system is part of the immune system and circulatory system, consisting of lymphatic vessels, lymph nodes, and lymphoid organs.

🔄 Functions of the Lymphatic System

  • Fluid balance - Returns interstitial fluid to bloodstream
  • Immune defense - Filters pathogens and produces immune cells
  • Fat absorption - Absorbs dietary fats in small intestine
  • Waste removal - Removes cellular waste and toxins

Terminology: Lymphatic

Lymph

Clear fluid that circulates through lymphatic vessels, containing WBCs and proteins.

Lymph Nodes

Small bean-shaped structures that filter lymph and produce lymphocytes.

Spleen

Largest lymphoid organ; filters blood, stores RBCs, and produces lymphocytes.

Thymus

Gland where T lymphocytes mature and become immunocompetent.

Tonsils

Lymphoid tissue in the throat that traps pathogens entering through mouth/nose.

Lymphocytes

White blood cells (T cells, B cells, NK cells) that mediate immune responses.

Lymphatic Disorders

Disorder Description Key Features
Lymphedema Swelling due to lymphatic obstruction Edema, heaviness, limited movement
Lymphoma Cancer of lymphatic system Enlarged lymph nodes, fever, weight loss
Hodgkin Disease Type of lymphoma with Reed-Sternberg cells Painless lymphadenopathy, B symptoms
Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma Various lymphomas without Reed-Sternberg cells Variable presentation, aggressive or indolent

⚠️ Must Know for Exam!

Hodgkin disease = Reed-Sternberg cells present
Non-Hodgkin = No Reed-Sternberg cells

🔬 Interactive Lymphoid Organs

Click on each organ to learn more:

Spleen Thymus Tonsils Lymph Nodes
Spleen - The LARGEST lymphoid organ, located in the left upper quadrant of the abdomen. Functions: filters blood (removes old/damaged RBCs), stores platelets and monocytes, produces lymphocytes, and initiates immune responses to blood-borne pathogens. Can be removed (splenectomy) if damaged, but patients have increased infection risk.
Thymus - A bilobed organ located in the mediastinum behind the sternum. PRIMARY function: T-cell maturation and differentiation. Most active during childhood and atrophies after puberty. T cells migrate here from bone marrow to become immunocompetent. Produces thymosin and other hormones.
Tonsils - Collections of lymphoid tissue surrounding the throat. Types: palatine (most common, removed in tonsillectomy), pharyngeal (adenoids), and lingual. Form a protective ring (Waldeyer's ring) against inhaled or ingested pathogens. Contain crypts that trap bacteria.
Lymph Nodes - Small, bean-shaped organs distributed throughout the body along lymphatic vessels. Filter lymph fluid before it returns to bloodstream. Contain B cells (cortex), T cells (paracortex), and macrophages. Enlargement (lymphadenopathy) indicates infection or malignancy.

🧠 Quick Knowledge Check

After a splenectomy, patients should receive which vaccinations?

Answer: Pneumococcal, Meningococcal, and Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) vaccines. Without a spleen, patients are at increased risk for encapsulated bacterial infections. Vaccinations should be given at least 2 weeks before elective splenectomy or as soon as possible after emergency surgery.

❤️ Chapter 12: Cardiovascular System

Heart Structure

Layer Description
Pericardium Fibrous sac surrounding the heart
Epicardium Outer layer of heart wall
Myocardium Middle muscular layer (contracts)
Endocardium Inner lining of heart chambers

Heart Valves

Valve Location Function
Tricuspid Right atrioventricular Prevents backflow from RV to RA
Pulmonary Right ventricle to pulmonary artery Prevents backflow from PA to RV
Mitral (Bicuspid) Left atrioventricular Prevents backflow from LV to LA
Aortic Left ventricle to aorta Prevents backflow from aorta to LV

Terminology: Cardiovascular

Cardiac Output

Volume of blood pumped by heart per minute (CO = HR × SV). Normal: 4-8 L/min.

Stroke Volume

Volume of blood ejected from ventricle per beat. Normal: 60-100 mL.

Ejection Fraction

Percentage of blood ejected from ventricle per beat. Normal: 55-70%.

Arrhythmia

Abnormal heart rhythm; can be too fast, too slow, or irregular.

Cardiovascular Disorders

Disorder Description Key Features
Heart Failure Inability to pump sufficient blood Edema, dyspnea, fatigue, JVD
Myocardial Infarction Heart attack - blocked coronary artery Chest pain, diaphoresis, nausea
Hypertension High blood pressure BP > 130/80 mmHg
Coronary Artery Disease Narrowing of coronary arteries Angina, decreased perfusion

Signs & Symptoms of Heart Failure

  • Left-sided HF: Pulmonary congestion, dyspnea, orthopnea, crackles in lungs
  • Right-sided HF: Systemic congestion, peripheral edema, JVD, hepatomegaly
  • General: Fatigue, weakness, decreased exercise tolerance

⚠️ Exam Tip!

Left HF = Lungs (pulmonary)
Right HF = Rest of body (systemic)

🔬 Interactive Heart Valves

Click on each valve to learn more:

Tricuspid Pulmonary Mitral Aortic
Tricuspid Valve - Located between the RIGHT atrium and RIGHT ventricle. Has 3 leaflets (cusps). Prevents backflow of blood from the right ventricle to the right atrium during ventricular contraction. Murmur heard best at the lower left sternal border.
Pulmonary Valve - Located between the RIGHT ventricle and PULMONARY ARTERY. A semilunar valve (3 cusps). Prevents backflow of blood from the pulmonary artery to the right ventricle. Murmur heard best at the upper left sternal border.
Mitral (Bicuspid) Valve - Located between the LEFT atrium and LEFT ventricle. Has 2 leaflets. Prevents backflow of blood from the left ventricle to the left atrium. The MOST COMMON site for valve disease. Murmur heard best at the apex (5th intercostal space, midclavicular line).
Aortic Valve - Located between the LEFT ventricle and AORTA. A semilunar valve (3 cusps). Prevents backflow of blood from the aorta to the left ventricle. Murmur heard best at the upper right sternal border. Critical for systemic circulation.

🧠 Quick Knowledge Check

Which heart valve is MOST commonly affected by rheumatic heart disease?

Answer: The Mitral valve. Rheumatic heart disease, caused by untreated streptococcal infections, most commonly affects the mitral valve (mitral stenosis). The aortic valve is the second most commonly affected. This can lead to valve scarring, stenosis, or regurgitation.

Respiratory Structures

Structure Function
Nasal cavity Warms, humidifies, filters air
Pharynx Passageway for air and food
Larynx Voice production, protects trachea
Trachea Windpipe; conducts air to bronchi
Bronchi Branch into bronchioles, conduct air

Terminology: Respiratory

Dyspnea

Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath.

Tachypnea

Rapid breathing (>20 breaths/min in adults).

Bradypnea

Slow breathing (<12 breaths/min in adults).

Apnea

Cessation of breathing for >10 seconds.

Orthopnea

Difficulty breathing when lying flat.

Cyanosis

Bluish discoloration due to low oxygen.

Respiratory Disorders

Disorder Description Key Features
COPD Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease Emphysema + chronic bronchitis
Asthma Reversible airway obstruction Wheezing, triggered by allergens
Pneumonia Infection of lung tissue Fever, cough, infiltrates on CXR
Pulmonary Embolism Blockage of pulmonary artery Sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia
Tuberculosis Bacterial infection (Mycobacterium) Chronic cough, night sweats, weight loss

Respiratory = CO2 problem

🔬 Interactive Respiratory Structures

Click on each structure to learn more:

Alveoli Diaphragm Larynx Bronchi
Alveoli - Tiny air sacs (300-500 million in lungs) where GAS EXCHANGE occurs. Type I pneumocytes (thin, allow diffusion), Type II pneumocytes (produce surfactant). Oxygen diffuses into blood; CO2 diffuses into alveoli to be exhaled. Damage causes emphysema.
Diaphragm - The MAIN muscle of respiration. Dome-shaped, separates thoracic and abdominal cavities. Contracts and flattens during inspiration (increases thoracic volume). Relaxes during expiration. Innervated by phrenic nerve (C3-C5). "C3, 4, 5 keep the diaphragm alive!"
Larynx (Voice Box) - Located between pharynx and trachea. Contains vocal cords for speech production. Protects lower airway from aspiration during swallowing. The epiglottis covers the larynx during swallowing. Cartilages: thyroid (Adam's apple), cricoid, arytenoid.
Bronchi & Bronchioles - Airways that conduct air to alveoli. Main bronchi branch into lobar, then segmental bronchi, then bronchioles. Bronchioles have smooth muscle (responsive to bronchodilators). In asthma, bronchioles constrict; in COPD, they lose elasticity.

🧠 Quick Knowledge Check

What is the key difference between asthma and COPD in terms of reversibility?

Answer: Asthma is REVERSIBLE; COPD is IRREVERSIBLE. Asthma episodes can be reversed with bronchodilators, and lung function can return to normal between attacks. COPD causes permanent airflow limitation that cannot be fully reversed, though symptoms can be managed with medications.
