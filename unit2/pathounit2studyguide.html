🩹 Chapter 8: Skin

Skin Structure Overview

The skin is the largest organ of the body, providing protection, temperature regulation, sensation, and vitamin D synthesis.

🔄 Layers of the Epidermis (Outer to Inner)

  • Stratum corneum - Outermost, keratinized dead cells
  • Stratum lucidum - Clear layer (only in thick skin)
  • Stratum granulosum - Keratin production begins
  • Stratum spinosum - Spiny appearance, Langerhans cells
  • Stratum basale (germinativum) - Deepest, stem cells, melanocytes

Terminology: Skin

Keratinocytes

Primary cells of the epidermis that produce keratin, making skin waterproof and resistant.

Melanocytes

Cells in the stratum basale that produce melanin, providing skin color and UV protection.

Langerhans Cells

Immune cells in the stratum spinosum that fight pathogens and present antigens.

Merkel Cells

Touch receptors located in the stratum basale, connected to nerve endings.

Epidermis Dermis Subcutaneous Appendages
Epidermis - The outermost layer of skin. Composed of stratified squamous epithelium. Contains 5 layers: stratum corneum (outermost), lucidum, granulosum, spinosum, and basale (innermost). No blood vessels - receives nutrients through diffusion from dermis. Key cells: keratinocytes (produce keratin), melanocytes (produce melanin), Langerhans cells (immune function).
Dermis - The middle layer, composed of connective tissue. Contains two layers: papillary (upper, loose connective tissue) and reticular (lower, dense connective tissue). Houses blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, sebaceous glands, and sweat glands. Provides strength and elasticity through collagen and elastin fibers.
Subcutaneous Tissue (Hypodermis) - The deepest layer. Composed primarily of adipose tissue and loose connective tissue. Functions: insulation, energy storage, shock absorption, and anchors skin to underlying structures. Contains larger blood vessels and nerves.
Skin Appendages - Include hair, nails, sebaceous glands, and sweat glands. Hair: provides protection and warmth. Nails: protect distal phalanges. Sebaceous glands: produce sebum (oily substance). Sweat glands: eccrine (thermoregulation) and apocrine (scent).

🧠 Quick Knowledge Check

What is the MOST important intervention for preventing pressure injuries in immobile patients?

Answer: Repositioning every 2 hours (or more frequently for high-risk patients). Other important interventions include: using pressure-relieving mattresses, keeping skin clean and dry, ensuring adequate nutrition (especially protein), and managing moisture from incontinence.

🦴 Chapter 9: Musculoskeletal System

Bone Cells

Cell Type Function Key Points
Osteoblasts Build new bone Form new bone matrix, "Blast = Build"
Osteoclasts Break down bone Resorb bone, release calcium, "Clast = Crush"
Osteocytes Maintain bone Mature cells in lacunae, detect stress
Osteogenic cells Stem cells Found in periosteum, differentiate into osteoblasts

⚠️ Exam Tip!

Remember: Blasts Build, Clasts Crush! Osteoblasts create new bone; osteoclasts resorb bone.

Terminology: Musculoskeletal

Periosteum

Fibrous membrane covering bone surfaces, contains blood vessels and osteogenic cells.

Endosteum

Thin membrane lining the inner bone surfaces and medullary cavity.

Lacunae

Small spaces in bone matrix where osteocytes reside.

Canaliculi

Tiny channels connecting lacunae, allowing nutrient exchange between osteocytes.

Fracture Types

Type Description
Closed/Simple Fracture doesn't break through skin
Open/Compound Fracture breaks through skin (higher infection risk)
Comminuted Bone shattered into multiple pieces
Greenstick Incomplete fracture (common in children)
Pathologic Due to bone disease (osteoporosis, cancer)

🔬 Interactive Bone Cell Functions

Click on each cell type to learn more:

Osteoblast Osteoclast Osteocyte Osteogenic
Osteoblasts (Bone Builders) - These cells synthesize and secrete collagen and other components of the bone matrix. They are responsible for bone formation and mineralization. When they become trapped in the matrix they produce, they differentiate into osteocytes. Memory trick: "Blast = Build"
Osteoclasts (Bone Breakers) - Large, multinucleated cells that resorb bone by releasing enzymes and acids. They break down bone tissue, releasing calcium and phosphate into the bloodstream. Essential for bone remodeling and calcium homeostasis. Memory trick: "Clast = Crush"
Osteocytes (Bone Maintainers) - Mature bone cells derived from osteoblasts that have become trapped in the bone matrix. They reside in lacunae and communicate through canaliculi. They detect mechanical stress and help regulate bone remodeling by signaling osteoblasts and osteoclasts.
Osteogenic Cells (Bone Stem Cells) - Undifferentiated stem cells found in the periosteum and endosteum. They can differentiate into osteoblasts when needed for bone repair or growth. These cells are essential for fracture healing and bone regeneration.

🧠 Quick Knowledge Check

What complication is MOST concerning in the first 24-48 hours after a long bone fracture?

Answer: Fat embolism syndrome (FES). This occurs when fat globules from the bone marrow enter the bloodstream, potentially causing respiratory distress, neurological changes, and petechial rash. Other early complications include compartment syndrome and deep vein thrombosis (DVT).

Fracture Healing Stages

  • Stage 1: Hematoma formation - Blood clot forms at fracture site
  • Stage 2: Soft callus formation - Fibrocartilage bridges the gap (weeks 1-2)
  • Stage 3: Hard callus formation - Osteoblasts create new bone (weeks 3-4)
  • Stage 4: Bone remodeling - Bone reshaped by osteoclasts/osteoblasts (months-years)

Signs of Fracture

The 5 P's

  • Pain at fracture site
  • Pallor (pale skin)
  • Paresthesia (numbness/tingling)
  • Pulselessness (absent pulse distal to fracture)
  • Paralysis (inability to move)

Other signs: Swelling, deformity, crepitus (grinding sound), bruising

Blood Components

Component Function Normal Values
Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes) Carry oxygen via hemoglobin Male: 4.7-6.1 million/μL
Female: 4.2-5.4 million/μL
White Blood Cells (Leukocytes) Immune defense 4,500-11,000/μL
Platelets (Thrombocytes) Blood clotting 150,000-400,000/μL
Plasma Transport medium (55% of blood volume) ~55% of total blood volume

Terminology: Blood

Hemoglobin (Hgb)

Iron-containing protein in RBCs that carries oxygen. Normal: Male 14-18 g/dL, Female 12-16 g/dL.

Hematocrit (Hct)

Percentage of blood volume occupied by RBCs. Normal: Male 42-52%, Female 37-47%.

Erythropoietin (EPO)

Hormone produced by kidneys that stimulates RBC production in bone marrow.

Bilirubin

Yellow pigment from hemoglobin breakdown; elevated in jaundice.

White Blood Cell Types

Type % of WBCs Function
Neutrophils 60-70% First responders to bacterial infection; phagocytosis
Lymphocytes 20-25% Viral defense; B cells (antibodies), T cells (cell-mediated)
Monocytes 3-8% Become macrophages; chronic infection fighters
Eosinophils 2-4% Allergic reactions; parasitic infections
Basophils 0.5-1% Release histamine in allergic reactions

Types of Anemia

Type Cause Key Features
Iron Deficiency Inadequate iron intake/absorption or blood loss Microcytic, hypochromic RBCs; fatigue, pallor
Pernicious Anemia Vitamin B12 deficiency (lack of intrinsic factor) Macrocytic RBCs; neurologic symptoms
Sickle Cell Anemia Genetic; abnormal hemoglobin S Sickled RBCs; vaso-occlusive crises
Aplastic Anemia Bone marrow failure Pancytopenia (low all cell types)
Hemolytic Anemia Premature RBC destruction Jaundice, splenomegaly

⚠️ Critical Point!

Iron deficiency = microcytic (small cells)
B12/folate deficiency = macrocytic (large cells)

Signs & Symptoms of Anemia

  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Pallor (pale skin, mucous membranes)
  • Shortness of breath (dyspnea)
  • Tachycardia (rapid heart rate)
  • Dizziness, headache
  • Cold hands and feet
  • Brittle nails

🔬 Interactive White Blood Cell Types

Click on each WBC type to learn more:

Neutrophil Lymphocyte Monocyte Eosinophil Basophil
Neutrophils (60-70%) - The most abundant WBCs and FIRST RESPONDERS to bacterial infections. They perform phagocytosis (engulf and destroy bacteria). Elevated in acute bacterial infections (left shift). Life span: 6-8 hours in blood, 1-2 days in tissue. Memory: "Neu-tro-phil" = "New" = First on scene!
Lymphocytes (20-25%) - Key players in viral defense and specific immunity. Two main types: B cells (produce antibodies) and T cells (cell-mediated immunity, CD4/CD8). Elevated in viral infections. Small cells with large, round nucleus that fills most of the cell.
Monocytes (3-8%) - The LARGEST WBCs. They circulate briefly then migrate to tissues and become MACROPHAGES. Excellent phagocytes for chronic infections and clean-up of cellular debris. Kidney-shaped nucleus. Elevated in chronic infections like TB.
Eosinophils (2-4%) - Elevated in ALLERGIC REACTIONS and PARASITIC INFECTIONS. Bilobed nucleus with red-orange granules. Release major basic protein and other enzymes to combat parasites. Also involved in asthma and allergic responses.
Basophils (0.5-1%) - The LEAST ABUNDANT WBCs. Release HISTAMINE and heparin. Similar function to mast cells. Involved in allergic and inflammatory responses. Dark blue/purple granules obscure the nucleus. Cause vasodilation and increased permeability.

🧠 Quick Knowledge Check

Which WBC type would be MOST elevated in a patient with a parasitic infection?

Answer: Eosinophils. These cells are specifically designed to combat parasitic infections and are also elevated in allergic reactions. They release enzymes that damage the parasite membrane. Normal range is only 2-4% of WBCs, but can increase significantly with parasites.
