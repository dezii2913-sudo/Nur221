⚠️ Carcinogenesis takes an extended period of time — it is NOT a sudden event. Multiple exposures across years before cancer fully develops.
Inherited (~5%)
Germline mutation (in sex cells). Cancer appears earlier in life. NOT guaranteed to get cancer — just higher risk. Example: BRCA1/2 → breast cancer risk.
Acquired (~95%)
Somatic mutation (body cells). Cancer appears later in life. Driven by environmental influences — lifestyle, exposure, carcinogens.

🔁 Proto-oncogene → Oncogene

Proto-oncogene (NORMAL ✅)
"Normal" genes that regulate cell growth and division. Every cell has them. They are ESSENTIAL for normal function.
Oncogene (MUTATED ❌)
When a proto-oncogene mutates → becomes an oncogene → drives uncontrolled growth. Like turning OFF the speed limiter on a car — no brakes.
🧠 Tumor Suppressor Genes: Regulate the rate at which cells divide and die (apoptosis). When mutated → cell loses its "off switch" → keeps dividing. Mutations can occur in germline OR somatic cells.

⭐ MUST KNOW ORDER: Initiation → Promotion → Progression. If promotion occurs BEFORE initiation, or either occurs in ISOLATION → cancer does NOT develop.
I — Initiation

Carcinogenic agent causes DNA damage and cell mutation. May be unknown event. Creates irreversible change but NO active neoplasm yet.

→
P — Promotion

Promoter agent causes further DNA changes → less differentiation + ↑ mitosis. Oncogenes are activated. Cell growth depends on how long promoter is present.

→
P — Progression

Growth is NOW independent of the promoting agent. Tumor grows on its own. Cells can detach and spread to distant sites (metastasis).

🧠 Memory Trick: "I Put Pepper on my food" — I=Initiation, P=Promotion, P=Progression
🚬 Smoking Example from Professor: Predisposition → Tobacco use (promoter) → Cell changes begin [STOP HERE = no cancer] → Prolonged use → Changes continue EVEN IF smoking stops → Lung cancer

IPP Diagram: Normal cell → Initiation (DNA damage) → Promotion (oncogene activation) → Progression → Malignant tumor

🧪 BRCA Gene Test: Tests for mutations in BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes — tumor suppressor genes. If mutated, greatly increases risk of breast and ovarian cancer. This is an example of GENOMIC testing to identify inherited cancer risk (germline mutation). A positive BRCA test = ↑ risk but NOT a guarantee of cancer.
Homozygous Dominant (AA)
Both alleles are dominant. Trait IS expressed. If dominant = disease, will have it.
Heterozygous (Aa)
One dominant, one recessive. For dominant disorders = HAVE the disease. For recessive disorders = CARRIER only.
Homozygous Recessive (aa)
Both alleles are recessive. For recessive disorders = HAVE the disease (e.g., cystic fibrosis, sickle cell). Two carriers (Aa × Aa) = 25% chance aa child.

📘 Chapter 20 — Detailed Professor Notes
🔬 Carcinogenesis — Full Lecture Notes
Carcinogenesis takes an extended period of time. Initiated by genetic mutations influenced by gene variants and epigenetics. The two key categories: Inherited (~5%, germline, earlier in life, not guaranteed) and Acquired (~95%, somatic, environmental, later in life).

Cell Regulators

Proto-oncogenes are "normal" genes that play a vital role in regulating cell function. They turn into an oncogene if mutated — leading to uncontrolled growth. Mostly in somatic cells.

Tumor Suppressor Genes regulate rate of cell division and apoptosis. Mutations can occur in germline or somatic cells.

Epigenetics (Professor Definition): How your behaviors and environment can cause changes that affect the way your genes work. Reversible — do NOT change the DNA sequence bases, but change HOW your body reads a DNA sequence.
Polymorphism Example: Affecting estrogen levels → contributing to hormone-dependent cancers. Not a mutation but sets the stage.

Direct vs. Indirect Carcinogens

Direct: Viruses (HPV → cervical, Hepatitis → liver) — cause modification of cell DNA directly.

Indirect: Tobacco smoke, certain foods, industrial chemicals, alcohol — induce immunosuppression or chronic inflammation.

🎯 IPP Theory — Full Professor Notes

Initiating factors (procarcinogens) cause the first irreversible changes in cell DNA. This initial change does NOT create an active neoplasm.

Exposure to promoters later causes further changes — less differentiation, increased mitosis. Dysplasia or anaplasia may be evident. Promoters include hormones and environmental chemicals. The prolonged time interval and multiple factors complicate research on risk factors.

Continued exposure results in a malignant tumor capable of growth, local invasion, and eventually metastasis (cells detach from tumor and spread to distant sites).

Smoking progression: Predisposition → tobacco use (promoter) → cell changes begin [STOP HERE = cancer won't develop] → prolonged use → changes continue even after stopping → lung cancer develops.
💊 Cachexia & Manifestations — Full Notes

Clinical manifestations of cancer can be local or systemic. Tissue necrosis and ulceration may lead to infection around the tumor — particularly in areas where normal flora can become opportunistic (e.g., oral cavity cancers).

Cachexia: Results from early feelings of fullness + release of chemical mediators (tumor necrosis factor) → lipid energy unavailable to tissues → promotes tissue wasting. Increased TNF = lipid energy unavailable → profound weight loss even with some intake.

Genomic assays may identify genetic mutations independent of heredity but only occurring with the disease itself — helps inform treatment decisions (personalized medicine).

🧬 Chapter 21 — Detailed Professor Notes
🔬 DNA/RNA — Full Notes

Pieces of DNA make up genes. The four nitrogenous bases (adenine, guanine, cytosine, thymine) are foundations for DNA base pairs forming the genetic code. RNA delivers DNA's genetic message to the cytoplasm where proteins are made. Proteins enable cells to carry out cellular processes and influence traits (eye color, height).

mRNA: Reads the DNA nucleotide bases, transcribes them into complementary RNA bases (like sounding out a word letter by letter)
tRNA: Recognizes each codon, binds to it in the ribosome, transfers specific amino acid to growing chain (each syllable comes together to make a word)

Amino acids combine to form polypeptide chains (words → sentences → paragraphs → whole books = proteins).

🧩 Inheritance Patterns — Full Notes

Most hereditary disorders involve autosome genes (22 pairs vs 1 sex pair) — affect males and females equally.

Autosomal Recessive probabilities: 2 carriers × 2 carriers = 25% affected, 50% carrier, 25% unaffected. Two affected parents = 100% affected.

X-linked: Father with defective X → all daughters are carriers, sons unaffected (father passes Y to sons). Mother carrier → daughters 50% carrier, sons 50% affected.

Mitochondrial: Sperm do NOT carry significant mitochondrial genes — males cannot pass mitochondrial diseases. Heteroplasmy = random distribution of mitochondria during embryonic cell division → variable manifestations.

Chromosomal Alterations detected by karyotype — a picture of number and visual appearance of chromosomes, matched by centromere location and banding pattern.

🤰 Chapter 22 — Detailed Professor Notes
🔄 Physiologic Changes — Full Notes

Hormonal: Estrogen and progesterone increase as placenta ramps up production — essential for uterine development, pregnancy maintenance, and breast preparation for lactation. Thyroid hyperplasia and ↑ thyroxine increases maternal metabolism.

Reproductive: Estrogen → uterus hypertrophy (muscle hypertrophy + hyperplasia + ↑ fibrous tissue + ↑ blood vessels). Cervical mucus more abundant/thick → cervical plug protects uterine contents.

Cardiovascular: Blood volume increases overall. HR may increase slightly. BP drops in 1st/2nd trimester, returns to normal in 3rd trimester. ↑ Blood volume → congestion + edema (nasal congestion, gingivitis, varicose veins).

Musculoskeletal: Pelvic joints relax as hormones prepare pelvis for delivery → loss of stability → waddling gait. Increased abdominal weight → center of gravity shifts → lordosis.

⚠️ Complications — Full Notes

Blood Clotting: Thromboembolism and PE are risks after delivery — especially with varicose veins or clotting tendencies. DIC is a serious complication of abruptio placentae and preeclampsia.

Rh Incompatibility: Rh-negative mother + Rh-positive child. Consequences (hemolysis of fetal RBCs) avoided by treating mother at delivery to prevent immune response. RhoGAM given at 28 weeks and within 72 hours of delivery.

Infections: Wound infections (episiotomy, C-section), STIs, UTIs, mastitis, yeast infections (from decreased vaginal pH + ↑ glycogen) — usually well managed with antibiotics.

Preeclampsia definition: BP ≥140/90 + proteinuria after 20 weeks. Add headache, visual changes, epigastric pain. Magnesium sulfate prevents seizures. Delivery is the ONLY cure.
