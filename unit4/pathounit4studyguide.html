⚠️ Carcinogenesis takes an extended period of time — it is NOT a sudden event. Multiple exposures across years before cancer fully develops.
Inherited (~5%)
Germline mutation (in sex cells). Cancer appears earlier in life. NOT guaranteed to get cancer — just higher risk. Example: BRCA1/2 → breast cancer risk.
Acquired (~95%)
Somatic mutation (body cells). Cancer appears later in life. Driven by environmental influences — lifestyle, exposure, carcinogens.

🔁 Proto-oncogene → Oncogene

Proto-oncogene (NORMAL ✅)
"Normal" genes that regulate cell growth and division. Every cell has them. They are ESSENTIAL for normal function.
Oncogene (MUTATED ❌)
When a proto-oncogene mutates → becomes an oncogene → drives uncontrolled growth. Like turning OFF the speed limiter on a car — no brakes.
🧠 Tumor Suppressor Genes: Regulate the rate at which cells divide and die (apoptosis). When mutated → cell loses its "off switch" → keeps dividing. Mutations can occur in germline OR somatic cells.

⭐ MUST KNOW ORDER: Initiation → Promotion → Progression. If promotion occurs BEFORE initiation, or either occurs in ISOLATION → cancer does NOT develop.
I — Initiation

Carcinogenic agent causes DNA damage and cell mutation. May be unknown event. Creates irreversible change but NO active neoplasm yet.

→
P — Promotion

Promoter agent causes further DNA changes → less differentiation + ↑ mitosis. Oncogenes are activated. Cell growth depends on how long promoter is present.

→
P — Progression

Growth is NOW independent of the promoting agent. Tumor grows on its own. Cells can detach and spread to distant sites (metastasis).

🧠 Memory Trick: "I Put Pepper on my food" — I=Initiation, P=Promotion, P=Progression
🚬 Smoking Example from Professor: Predisposition → Tobacco use (promoter) → Cell changes begin [STOP HERE = no cancer] → Prolonged use → Changes continue EVEN IF smoking stops → Lung cancer

IPP Diagram: Normal cell → Initiation (DNA damage) → Promotion (oncogene activation) → Progression → Malignant tumor

⭐ Cachexia is NOT just "loss of appetite" — it is a profound metabolic wasting syndrome driven by Tumor Necrosis Factor (TNF). Even if the patient eats, they continue to waste.
Cachexia Mechanism
1. Early feelings of fullness with eating
2. TNF + other chemical mediators released
3. TNF → lipid energy UNAVAILABLE to tissues
4. Promotes severe tissue wasting
Anorexia vs. Cachexia
Anorexia = loss of appetite from inflammatory mediators (input problem)
Cachexia = metabolic wasting from TNF even WITH some intake (utilization problem)

🧠 Palliative Care = COMFORT CARE ONLY. NOT curing, NOT extending life — it is about reducing suffering and improving quality of life. Patient who says "I hope this limits my discomfort" = CORRECT understanding. "This will help me live longer" = INCORRECT — palliative does NOT postpone death.

🧪 BRCA Gene Test: Tests for mutations in BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes — tumor suppressor genes. If mutated, greatly increases risk of breast and ovarian cancer. Homozygous Dominant (AA)

Heterozygous (Aa)

Homozygous Recessive (aa)
Both alleles are recessive. For recessive disorders = HAVE the disease (e.g., cystic fibrosis, sickle cell). Two carriers (Aa × Aa) = 25% chance aa child.

🚨 Ectopic = pregnancy implantation OUTSIDE the uterus, most commonly in the FALLOPIAN TUBES (95% of cases)
Why Dangerous
Fallopian tube cannot expand like the uterus. As embryo grows → tube stretches → can RUPTURE → massive internal bleeding → life-threatening emergency.
Signs & Symptoms
• Missed period + positive pregnancy test
• Unilateral pelvic pain (sharp, cramping)
• Vaginal spotting/bleeding
• If rupture: severe abdominal pain, shoulder pain (diaphragm irritation from blood), hypotension, shock
Risk factors: Previous ectopic pregnancy, PID (scarring), tubal surgery, IUD use, smoking. Treatment: Methotrexate (medical) OR surgical removal (salpingectomy).

Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM): Insulin resistance caused by placental hormones (human placental lactogen, progesterone, cortisol). Usually develops in 2nd/3rd trimester. Resolves after delivery but ↑ risk of future Type 2 DM (50% in 10 years). Screening: 1-hour glucose challenge at 24–28 weeks. Risks: macrosomia (big baby → shoulder dystocia), neonatal hypoglycemia, preeclampsia.
Hyperemesis Gravidarum: SEVERE, persistent nausea and vomiting during pregnancy that is beyond normal morning sickness. Leads to: dehydration, electrolyte imbalances (especially hypokalemia), weight loss, and may require IV fluids and hospitalization. Distinguished from normal N/V by: inability to keep any food/fluid down + weight loss + metabolic disturbances.
Rh Incompatibility & RhoGAM: Rh-negative mother + Rh-positive fetus → maternal immune system attacks fetal RBCs (hemolysis). RhoGAM (Rh immunoglobulin) is given at 28 weeks AND within 72 hours of delivery to PREVENT sensitization. If sensitization occurs → affects SUBSEQUENT pregnancies (not usually the first).
🧒 Chapter 23 — Development & Aging
⭐ Teacher Emphasis: Telomere shortening drives aging • Puberty trigger = hypothalamus → pituitary → gonads
Metabolic syndrome = obesity + insulin resistance + hypertension • Osteoporosis: NOT caused by ↓PTH
Sarcopenia = muscle loss • Senescence = cell death outpaces replication • Alopecia = hair loss
Puberty Trigger — Hypothalamus: The hypothalamus initiates puberty by secreting GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone) → stimulates pituitary → LH & FSH released → gonads produce sex hormones (estrogen/testosterone). Without hypothalamic signaling, puberty cannot begin.
Female Puberty Sequence: Breast development (thelarche) → pubic hair (pubarche) → growth spurt → menarche (first period). Average onset: 8–13 years. Girls' growth spurt peaks BEFORE menarche.
Adolescent Pregnancy Risk Factors: Teen girls face increased pregnancy risk due to: early sexual activity without contraception, reluctance to tell parents (fear/shame), poor nutrition (skipping meals), failure to take prenatal vitamins, late or no prenatal care. These compound fetal and maternal health risks significantly.
Epiphyseal (Growth) Plate Damage: The epiphyseal plate (physis) at the ends of long bones is where longitudinal growth occurs. Damage to this plate during adolescence — from fracture, trauma, or infection — can prematurely close the growth plate and permanently stunt bone growth/limb length discrepancy.
Juvenile Rheumatoid Arthritis (JRA) — AUTOIMMUNE: JRA is an autoimmune disease in which the body's immune system attacks synovial joints. Chronic inflammation leads to joint damage, pain, stiffness, and swelling. Onset before age 16. Systemic JRA (Still's disease) can affect internal organs. Treatment: NSAIDs, DMARDs, biologics.
Anorexia Nervosa: Self-starvation in the absence of another disease — this distinguishes it from malnutrition due to illness. Primarily affects adolescent females. Characterized by severely restricted food intake, distorted body image, intense fear of weight gain. Complications: electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia), lanugo hair growth, amenorrhea, osteoporosis, cardiac arrhythmias.
Bulimia Nervosa: Binge eating followed by purging behaviors (vomiting, laxatives, excessive exercise). Key distinguishing sign: dental caries (cavities) from repeated exposure of teeth to gastric acid. Also: parotid gland swelling, Russell's sign (knuckle calluses), electrolyte disturbances (hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis from vomiting).
Scoliosis — How to Assess: Abnormal lateral curvature of the spine. Assessment uses the Adam's Forward Bend Test: patient bends forward — examiner looks at the hips and rib cage asymmetry, NOT directly at the back surface. A rib hump (posterior prominence) or hip asymmetry indicates scoliosis. Screening at 10–14 years old.
Metabolic Syndrome: A cluster of metabolic disorders occurring together that dramatically increases cardiovascular risk. Requires ≥3 of: central obesity (waist >35" F / >40" M), hypertriglyceridemia, low HDL, hypertension, elevated fasting glucose. Think of it as having ALL types of metabolic diseases at once.
Down Syndrome — MUST KNOW Features:
  • Protruding tongue (macroglossia + hypotonia of facial muscles)
  • Congenital heart disease — most common cause of death (AV canal defects most frequent)
  • Limited mental development — intellectual disability ranges from mild to moderate
  • Single palmar crease (simian crease), upslanting palpebral fissures, epicanthal folds
  • Trisomy 21 — extra chromosome 21 (nondisjunction during meiosis)
  • Increased risk of leukemia, Alzheimer's disease (early onset)
  • Atlanto-axial instability — neck must be protected
Senescence: The state in which cell destruction outpaces replication. Cells lose their ability to divide (due to DNA damage, telomere shortening, etc.) and accumulate, releasing inflammatory signals. Senescent cells are a key driver of aging and age-related disease.
Telomere Shortening & Aging: Telomeres are protective caps on chromosome ends. With each cell division, telomeres shorten. When critically short, cells undergo senescence or apoptosis (cannot divide further). This is a primary molecular clock of aging. Telomerase can rebuild telomeres — active in stem cells and cancer cells; largely inactive in somatic cells.
Life Span vs. Life Expectancy: Life span = the maximum possible age a species can reach (humans ~120 years). Life expectancy = the average age a person in a given population is expected to live, influenced by healthcare, environment, genetics, socioeconomic status. Life expectancy varies; life span is relatively fixed.
Factors Impacting Growth (Expanded):
  • Genetics — primary determinant of final height potential
  • Nutrition — protein, calories, zinc, iron essential; malnutrition stunts growth
  • Hormones — GH (growth hormone), IGF-1, thyroid hormone, sex steroids
  • Vitamin D — NOT solely for bone; also critical for immune function, muscle function, mood regulation, and calcium absorption
  • Sleep — GH secreted in pulses during deep sleep; poor sleep = impaired growth in children
  • Exercise — weight-bearing exercise promotes bone density; excessive exercise in adolescence can suppress puberty (female athlete triad)
  • Chronic illness — inflammatory diseases, chronic hypoxia, renal failure all impair growth
Common Changes in Aging:
  • Cardiovascular: ↑ arterial stiffness, ↑ systolic BP, ↓ cardiac reserve
  • Pulmonary: ↓ elastic recoil, ↑ residual volume, ↓ FEV1
  • Renal: ↓ GFR (~1% per year after age 40), ↓ ability to concentrate urine
  • Musculoskeletal: Sarcopenia (↓ muscle mass), osteoporosis, ↓ bone density
  • Neurological: ↓ nerve conduction velocity, ↓ proprioception, ↑ fall risk
  • Immune: Immunosenescence — ↓ T-cell function, ↑ susceptibility to infection and cancer
  • Reproductive — Females: Menopause = clinically defined as NO period for 12 consecutive months (book says ~50, but true diagnosis = 1 full year without period)
  • Reproductive — Males & Females: Both experience decreased fertility with aging; males see ↓ testosterone + ↓ sperm quality; females see ↓ ovarian reserve
  • GI: ↓ gastric motility, ↓ saliva production, ↑ constipation risk
  • Skin: ↓ collagen, ↑ fragility, ↓ wound healing, ↓ vitamin D synthesis

📘 Chapter 20 — Neoplasms & Cancer (Full Professor Notes)
🔬 Cell Biology Foundations
⭐ PROFESSOR FOCUS: Understand labile vs. permanent cells — labile cells are MOST prone to neoplasms.

Proliferation — Generation of new daughter cells divided from progenitor (parent) cells. Normal and regulated in healthy tissue.

Differentiation — The orderly process of cellular maturation to achieve a specific function. Well-differentiated = normal-looking cells. Poorly differentiated = anaplastic cancer cells.

Stem Cells — Highly undifferentiated units that have the potential to divide into progenitor cells, then daughter cells, which can mature into more differentiated units with specific functions.

Labile Cells — Rapidly dividing, easily altered cells such as epithelial cells and blood cells. HIGHLY PRONE to neoplasm development because they divide so frequently.

Permanent Cells — Non-dividing cells such as cardiac myocytes, mature neurons, and lens of the eye. LESS PRONE to neoplasm development because they don't divide.

Neoplasms — Cell clusters that ignore genetic controls on proliferation and differentiation. Uncontrolled and unregulated. Can be benign OR malignant.

Cancer — Caused by altering genes that control cell reproduction, growth, differentiation, and/or death. Highly invasive and destructive neoplasms.

🧬 Carcinogenesis — Origin & Development
🎯 EXAM GUARANTEED: Carcinogenesis begins with an IRREVERSIBLE CHANGE in cell DNA.

Carcinogenesis — The origin and development of cancerous neoplasms. Influenced by genetic mutations, gene variants, and epigenetics.

Inherited cancers — ~5% of all cancers. Germline mutation (present in every cell). Cancer occurs earlier in life. NOT guaranteed to get cancer just because you carry the mutation.

Acquired cancers — ~95% of all cancers. Somatic mutation (in body cells, not reproductive). Occur later in life. Environmental influences are major contributors.

Proto-oncogene — A "normal" gene that plays a vital role in regulating cell function (growth, division, death). Think of it as the accelerator pedal — works normally until mutated.

Oncogene — A mutated proto-oncogene. When activated, it drives uncontrolled cell growth and division — the accelerator pedal is stuck down. Mostly in somatic cells.

Tumor Suppressor Genes — Genes that regulate the rate at which cells divide and die (apoptosis). They are the BRAKE pedal. Mutations can occur in germline or somatic cells. Both copies must be inactivated (two-hit hypothesis) for cancer to develop.

Epigenetics — Turning genes on and off WITHOUT changing the DNA sequence itself. REVERSIBLE changes to gene expression. Methods include: DNA methylation, histone modification, RNA interference. Can alter parts of genes that control cell growth.

Polymorphisms — Inherited differences in genes that are NOT mutations but can still impact cell growth and division. Do not cause cancer directly but can set the stage for neoplasm development.

📐 Initiation–Promotion–Progression (IPP) Theory

Initiation — Causes a mutation in a cell (event/exposure may be unknown). This is the FIRST irreversible DNA change. If initiation occurs alone (without promotion), cancer does NOT develop.

Promotion — Expansion of the mutated cell's growth and reproduction. Oncogenes are activated here. This stage is REVERSIBLE — it depends on how long the promoter is present. If promotion occurs BEFORE initiation, cancer does NOT develop.

Progression — Extension of promotion phase but growth is NO LONGER dependent on the promoting agent — independent growth. This is when the tumor becomes invasive and can metastasize.

⚠️ Carcinogens — Cancer-Causing Agents

Carcinogen — Any agent that interferes with molecular pathways and can initiate or promote tumor formation.

Direct carcinogens — Cause DIRECT modification of cell DNA. Interfere with cell function immediately WITHOUT needing metabolic activation. Examples: HPV (human papillomavirus), ionizing radiation, certain chemotherapy agents.

Indirect carcinogens — Induce immunosuppression or chronic inflammation, OR act in conjunction with other carcinogens to induce DNA damage. Require metabolic conversion before damaging DNA. Examples: tobacco, alcohol, benzene, nitrosamines in processed foods.

🤒 Manifestations of Cancer
🎯 EXAM: CAUTION warning signs — ALL of these are warning signs together.

CAUTION Acronym — Warning signs of cancer: C = Changes in bowel or bladder habits A = A sore that does not heal U = Unusual bleeding or discharge T = Thickening or lump in breast or elsewhere I = Indigestion or difficulty swallowing O = Obvious changes in a wart or mole N = Nagging cough or hoarseness

Asymptomatic — Many cancers are first noted on imaging before any symptoms appear.

Lymphadenopathy — Hyperplasia of lymph nodes from lymphocyte activity fighting the tumor.

Fever — Results from pyrogens released directly from cancer cells and cells in the inflammatory response.

Anorexia — Results from inflammatory response caused by circulating chemical mediators.

🔑 CACHEXIA — SEVERE TISSUE WASTING (Professor said to absolutely know this!)

Cachexia — Results from two mechanisms: (1) Early feelings of fullness with eating + (2) Release of chemical mediators including Tumor Necrosis Factor (TNF-α). TNF-α makes lipid energy UNAVAILABLE to tissues and actively promotes tissue wasting. CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Unlike starvation, cachexia is NOT reversed by increasing caloric intake — the metabolism itself is poisoned by the tumor.

Palliative Treatment — Intended to reduce manifestations and complications related to cancer and prolong life. There is NO curative intent. Goal is comfort and quality of life.

🧬 Chromosomes & Cell Division

Chromosomes — Composed of double-stranded DNA containing threadlike sections of genes. 46 total (23 pairs) in humans. Located in cell nucleus. Reproduce their physical and chemical structures, passing on genetic information during cell division.

23 pairs total: 22 pairs = autosomes (non-sex chromosomes) + 1 pair = sex chromosomes (XX = female, XY = male)

Genotype — The combination of ova and sperm at fertilization that determines the genetic makeup of an individual. The actual DNA code.

Phenotype — Traits that are apparent or OBSERVABLE (think PHYSICAL traits). Examples: blood type, eye color, hair color. Same genotype can produce different phenotypes depending on environment.

Alleles — Two or more different genes occupying the same location on a specific chromosome. Responsible for determining offspring traits.

Homozygous — Identical alleles on each chromosome (AA or aa).

Heterozygous — Two DIFFERENT alleles on each chromosome (Aa) — one copy for that gene.

🧩 Chromosomal Mutations

Deletion — A DNA segment is LOST entirely. All genes on that segment are missing.

Duplication — A DNA segment is copied and repeated. Increases gene dosage.

Inversion — A DNA segment breaks off and reattaches BACKWARDS on the SAME chromosome.

Insertion — Extra nucleotides are added. Causes a frameshift of all downstream codons.

Translocation — A large segment of DNA breaks from one chromosome and reattaches to a DIFFERENT chromosome. A balanced translocation carrier may be unaffected but offspring can receive unbalanced chromosomes with missing or extra genes.

Nondisjunction — Chromosomes FAIL to separate during meiosis. Results in gametes with too many or too few chromosomes (aneuploidy).

Monosomy — One copy of a chromosome instead of two. Involves gametes (otherwise cell dies). Example: Turner syndrome (45,X).

Trisomy — Three copies of a chromosome. Depends on which chromosome (large chromosome → cell death). Example: Trisomy 21 = Down syndrome.

📊 Inheritance Patterns
⭐ Based on Gregor Mendel's predictable patterns of inheritance.

Autosomal Dominant — Mutation on 1 dominant allele → expression of trait. ONE copy is enough. Each child of affected parent has 50% risk. Example: Huntington's disease, Marfan syndrome.

Autosomal Recessive — Mutation on 1 recessive allele → CARRIER status (no disease). Mutation on BOTH recessive alleles → expression of disease. Example: Cystic fibrosis (CFTR mutation), PKU, sickle cell disease. Two carriers → 25% chance affected child.

X-linked Dominant vs. Recessive — Mostly X-linked, therefore MEN are impacted more (only one X chromosome — no backup copy). Carrier females (Xx) have 50% chance of affected sons.

Mitochondrial Gene Disorders — Malfunctioning mitochondria. MATRILINEAL inheritance (female gametes contain more mitochondria). Variable manifestations related to heteroplasmy.

Polygenic/Multifactorial Disorders — Multiple alleles at different loci affecting phenotype. Less predictable. Genes + environment required. Examples: cleft lip/palate, clubfoot, neural tube defects (birth); hypertension, CAD, diabetes, many cancers (later in life).

🦠 Congenital Disorders & TORCH

Congenital disorders — Diseases or physical abnormalities present at birth. Gene-environment interactions from fertilization until birth.

Risk is GREATEST during embryologic period — Weeks 3 through 8 of gestation (organogenesis — organ formation).

TORCH infections — Pathogens that cross the placenta and cause severe fetal damage: T = Toxoplasmosis O = Other (syphilis, HIV, varicella) R = Rubella C = Cytomegalovirus (CMV) H = Herpes simplex virus

Other teratogens — Drugs, alcohol, environmental chemicals, ionizing radiation, maternal nutritional deficiencies.

Folic acid — PROTECTIVE against neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly). It is the EXCEPTION — it PREVENTS developmental disorders, it does NOT cause them.

🤱 Chapter 22 — Complications of Pregnancy (Full Professor Notes)
🌸 Physiologic Changes of Normal Pregnancy

Gravidity — Total number of pregnancies. Primigravida = pregnant for the first time. Multigravida = more than one pregnancy.

Parity — Number of pregnancies in which the fetus reached viability (~22 weeks gestation). Multipara = completed two or more viable pregnancies.

Hormonal Changes — Increased estrogen and progesterone. hCG maintains corpus luteum for first 8-10 weeks. Progesterone causes vasodilation (↓ BP), relaxes smooth muscle (GI slowing), maintains uterine lining.

Chadwick Sign — Typical deeper purplish color of the vaginal mucosal lining — due to increased vascularity.

Goodell Sign — Increased vascularity of cervix and vagina → softening of cervical tissues.

Uterine hypertrophy — Uterus grows dramatically to accommodate the fetus.

Cardiovascular changes — Blood volume increases 40-50%. Heart rate increases. Blood pressure may decrease slightly (vasodilation from progesterone). GFR increases ~50%.

GI changes — Nausea, vomiting, constipation, GERD/heartburn from uterus compressing stomach.

Musculoskeletal — Waddling gait, lordosis (exaggerated lumbar curve).

⚠️ Complications of Pregnancy
🎯 EXAM FOCUS: Know the distinction between placenta previa vs. abruptio placentae.

Ectopic Pregnancy (tubal pregnancy) — Fertilized ovum implants OUTSIDE the uterus. 95% in fallopian tube. Presents with unilateral pain, possible rupture and hemorrhage. Emergency.

Preeclampsia — BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks WITH proteinuria ± headache, visual changes, RUQ pain. The triad: hypertension + proteinuria + edema.

Eclampsia — Preeclampsia PLUS grand mal seizures. Medical emergency. Magnesium sulfate prevents/stops seizures.

HELLP Syndrome — Hemolysis + Elevated Liver enzymes + Low Platelets. Severe preeclampsia variant. Life-threatening.

Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM) — Increased glucose intolerance during pregnancy. Develops in 2-5% of women. Results in fetal hyperinsulinism → MACROSOMIA (high birth weight). Risk of delivery complications.

Placenta Previa — Placenta implanted over or near the cervical os. As uterus expands near end of pregnancy, placenta is torn → PAINLESS BRIGHT RED bleeding. Requires C-section. NO vaginal examination.

Abruptio Placentae — PREMATURE SEPARATION of placenta from uterine wall. Bleeding may be vaginal (dark red) OR concealed. Abdominal pain is common. Board-like rigid uterus. Major risk of DIC. Occurs more commonly in last trimester.

DIC in Pregnancy — Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation. Indicated by LOW serum clotting factors. Caused by thromboplastin release from abruptio placentae → massive clotting → factors consumed → uncontrolled bleeding.

Rh Incompatibility — Rh-negative mother, Rh-positive fetus. First pregnancy: sensitization (antibodies form). SECOND+ pregnancies: antibodies cross placenta → attack fetal RBCs → hemolytic disease of the newborn (erythroblastosis fetalis). Prevention: RhoGAM injection.

Hyperemesis Gravidarum — Severe uncontrollable vomiting (more than normal morning sickness). May cause dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and nutritional deficiency during critical fetal development.

Edema in Pregnancy — Develops because of increased blood volume (40-50% increase) → ↑ hydrostatic pressure in capillaries → fluid pushed into tissues. Normal physiological edema.

Neonatal Blood Tests — Performed on neonates shortly after birth to IDENTIFY metabolic and genetic disorders requiring IMMEDIATE treatment (PKU, hypothyroidism, galactosemia). Prevents irreversible damage.

🧒 Chapter 23 — Development & Aging (Full Professor Notes)
📈 Adolescent Development
⭐ Biological changes of adolescence are stimulated primarily by the PITUITARY GLAND (via gonadotropins).

Adolescence — Begins around age 10-12 years. Continues until physical growth is completed.

Puberty — The period when adolescents reach sexual maturity and become capable of reproduction.

Female puberty sequence — Pubic/axillary hair → breast development → growth spurt (EARLY in puberty, before menarche) → menarche (first period).

Male puberty sequence — Pubic/axillary hair → testicular enlargement → voice deepening → growth spurt (LATER in puberty, Tanner stage 4).

Factors impacting growth: Secretion of gonadotropins & sex hormones → musculoskeletal changes + sexual maturation. Linear growth (limb lengthening), widening pelvis (females), broadening shoulders/chest (males), muscle mass development.

Nutrition needs — Higher demands but often erratic eating patterns. Vitamin D and calcium critically important for bone development.

Genetic factors — Polygenic traits (genes + environment). Activity levels affect cardiovascular and pulmonary function.

⚠️ Complications of Adolescence
🎯 EXAM: Obesity in adolescents = ALL of these (physical, metabolic, psychological).

Obesity — Ability to burn fat during exercise DECLINES after puberty. Impacts: self-image, DM type 2 risk, cardiovascular disease, joint pain/damage. BMI at or above the 85th percentile = overweight; 95th percentile = obese.

Metabolic Syndrome — Presence of significant abdominal fat mass + changes in glucose metabolism + changes in lipoprotein metabolism. May also include changes in cardiac output, hypertension, or type 2 diabetes. THREE key factors: BMI ≥85th percentile + glucose/DM2 changes + hypertension.

Insulin Resistance — Body's cells become resistant to insulin effects → metabolic changes. Precursor to type 2 diabetes. Common in adolescent obesity.

Musculoskeletal complications — Postural problems (kyphosis = hunchback, lordosis = swayback), scoliosis (structural = fixed vs. functional = correctable), JRA (juvenile rheumatoid arthritis — autoimmune, affects large joints: knees, wrists, elbows).

Anorexia Nervosa — Extreme weight loss from self-starvation in the absence of another disease. Features: body image distortions, ignoring hunger, weight loss. INCREASED cortisol from stress of malnutrition. Risk of hypokalemia affecting cardiac function.

Bulimia Nervosa — Binge eating (especially carbohydrates) followed by purging. Can maintain normal weight. Risk of HYPOKALEMIA → cardiac arrhythmia.

Alopecia — Hair loss. Can be partial or complete. Caused by aging, hormonal changes, autoimmune conditions, chemotherapy.

Acne — Skin complication of adolescence from increased sebaceous gland activity.

Mononucleosis — Infectious complication common in adolescence (Epstein-Barr virus).

Klinefelter syndrome (XXY) — Small testes, no sperm, no secondary sex characteristics. May become obvious at puberty.

Turner syndrome (45,X) — Retarded growth, primary amenorrhea. May become obvious at puberty when normal development doesn't occur.

Testicular tumors — Peak incidence in adolescence/young adulthood.

🕐 Theories of Aging

Aging — Gradual deterioration of a mature organism resulting from time-dependent, irreversible changes in structure. Begins after birth.

Senescence — Period of life from old age to death. Cell destruction outpaces replication. Often used interchangeably with aging. Refers to postmaturational processes that lead to diminished homeostasis and increased vulnerability.

Life Expectancy — The age at which 50% of a given population is expected to survive.

Life Span — The MAXIMUM length of time a person can live.

📚 Theories on Aging

1. Apoptosis Theory — Programmed cell death is built in. Cells are genetically programmed to die after a certain number of divisions.

2. Wear and Tear Theory — Cumulative cellular damage from use over time, similar to how machines wear out. Free radicals and oxidative stress contribute.

3. Random Errors Theory — Errors occur during cell division that accumulate over time, gradually impairing cell function.

4. Telomere Shortening Theory — Telomeres (protective caps at chromosome ends) shorten with EACH cell division. When critically short, cells enter senescence (permanently stop dividing) or undergo apoptosis. Telomerase (active in stem cells and cancer cells) can rebuild telomeres. This is the cellular "biological clock."

5. Latent Viruses / Autoimmune Reactions — Latent viruses reactivate with aging as immune function declines. Autoimmune reactions increase as the immune system loses ability to distinguish self from non-self.

🦴 Osteoporosis — Predisposing Factors
⚠️ EXAM: PTH DECREASED is NOT a risk factor. Low PTH = less bone resorption = protective.

Risk factors FOR osteoporosis: Decreased estrogen (post-menopause — #1 in women), prolonged corticosteroid use, sedentary lifestyle, low calcium and vitamin D intake, smoking, excessive alcohol, small body frame, female sex, advancing age, family history.

PTH clarification: Parathyroid hormone INCREASES bone resorption (breaks down bone to release calcium). Therefore DECREASED PTH = LESS resorption. It is NOT a predisposing factor for osteoporosis.

Treatment/Prevention: Maintaining walking and weight-bearing exercise, adequate calcium/vitamin D, bisphosphonates (e.g., alendronate), avoiding excessive glucocorticoids.
