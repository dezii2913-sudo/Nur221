🚨 Cushing's Triad - Deep Dive (NOT the same as Cushing's Syndrome!)

What it is: A cluster of three LATE vital-sign changes that signal dangerously high intracranial pressure (ICP). By the time you see all three, brain herniation may be imminent — it is NOT an "early warning," it's a red alert.

First — don't confuse these:

Cushing's TRIADCushing's SYNDROME
3 vital signs from ↑ICP (brain emergency)Excess cortisol (endocrine disorder)
Named after neurosurgeon Harvey CushingAlso named after Harvey Cushing
Appears in brain injury, stroke, tumor, edemaMoon face, buffalo hump, striae

The 3 components — and why each happens:

SignSpecific FindingWhy It Occurs
1. BradycardiaHR drops (often below 60, sometimes 40s-50s)Brainstem compression triggers vagal response; baroreceptors sense the rising BP and slow the heart to try to lower pressure
2. Irregular respirationsCheyne-Stokes, ataxic, or apneustic breathing; may include apnea periodsRespiratory centers in medulla/pons are compressed; loss of normal respiratory rhythm control
3. Widened pulse pressureSystolic rises, diastolic stays same or drops (e.g., 180/60 = pulse pressure of 120)Body dramatically raises systolic BP to force blood past the rising ICP and perfuse the brain — "Cushing reflex"
🎯 NCLEX Traps with Cushing's Triad
  • HR and BP move OPPOSITE directions. In most shock states, HR goes UP when BP goes down. Here, BP goes UP while HR goes DOWN. If a question shows bradycardia + rising systolic BP = think ICP, not shock.
  • Pulse pressure, not MAP is what widens. Watch for answer choices that say "narrowed pulse pressure" — that's a distractor and is actually a sign of hypovolemic shock.
  • It's a LATE sign. If an NCLEX question asks for the EARLIEST sign of ↑ICP, the answer is NOT Cushing's triad — it's a change in level of consciousness (LOC). Restlessness, confusion, irritability, and headache come first.
  • Don't wait for all 3. Report and intervene on any single component in a patient at risk.

Early vs Late progression of ↑ICP — the full picture:

StageFindings
EarliestChange in LOC (restlessness, confusion, irritability, drowsiness)
EarlyHeadache (often worse in morning), nausea, projectile vomiting, blurred vision
ProgressingPupil changes (sluggish → unilateral dilation), motor weakness, seizures
LATE (Cushing's Triad)Bradycardia + Irregular respirations + Widened pulse pressure
TerminalFixed, dilated pupils; posturing (decorticate → decerebrate → flaccid); brain herniation; death

Immediate nursing actions when you see Cushing's triad:

  1. Notify provider/rapid response immediately
  2. Elevate HOB to 30° (neutral head position — no flexion/rotation)
  3. Prepare for intubation and mechanical ventilation (mild hyperventilation to PaCO2 30-35 reduces ICP short-term)
  4. Prepare mannitol or hypertonic saline for administration
  5. Avoid stimuli that spike ICP: Valsalva, coughing, suctioning beyond 10 seconds, clustering care
  6. Continuous neuro checks; prepare for emergent CT

Parkinson's Disease - Deep Dive

🧠 Parkinson's Disease

Pathophysiology: Progressive degeneration of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra (part of the basal ganglia). Loss of dopamine means unopposed acetylcholine activity → movement abnormalities. Symptoms usually appear when ~60-80% of dopamine neurons are already lost.

Classic signs: TRAP
Tremor • Rigidity • Akinesia/bradykinesia • Postural instability

Breaking down each sign:

  • Tremor: "Pill-rolling" resting tremor (worse at rest, improves with movement) — opposite of cerebellar tremor
  • Rigidity: "Cogwheel" resistance throughout passive ROM; feels ratchet-like
  • Akinesia/Bradykinesia: Slow initiation and execution of movement; shuffling gait, decreased arm swing, masked facies (flat facial expression)
  • Postural instability: Stooped posture, falls, "festination" (speeds up while walking as if chasing own center of gravity)

Other signs: Micrographia (small handwriting), drooling, dysphagia, soft voice (hypophonia), dementia in late stages

🎯 Parkinson's Medications — NCLEX Hot Topics
DrugKey Teaching
Levodopa-Carbidopa (Sinemet)Gold standard. Avoid high-protein meals (compete for absorption). "On-off" phenomenon over time. Report darkening of urine/sweat (harmless). May cause orthostatic hypotension — rise slowly.
Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole)Watch for sudden-onset sleep and impulse control issues (gambling, hypersexuality).
MAO-B inhibitors (selegiline)Avoid tyramine foods (aged cheese, cured meats, wine) — hypertensive crisis risk.
Anticholinergics (benztropine)For tremor. Dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention — avoid in elderly.

Nursing priorities: Fall precautions, speech/swallow therapy referrals, high-calorie nutrition, encourage movement, emotional support (depression common)

Myasthenia Gravis (MG) - Deep Dive

🧠 Myasthenia Gravis

Pathophysiology: Autoimmune disorder. Antibodies attack acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction → fewer receptors available → muscle weakness. Associated with thymus gland abnormalities (thymoma in ~15%, thymic hyperplasia in ~65%).

Hallmark feature: Weakness that worsens with activity and improves with rest. "I'm fine in the morning but exhausted by dinner."

Classic presentation:

  • Ocular first (early): Ptosis (drooping eyelids), diplopia (double vision) — often the first signs
  • Bulbar: Dysphagia, dysarthria (slurred speech), weakness of chewing/facial expression ("snarl" appearance when smiling)
  • Generalized: Proximal limb weakness (combing hair, climbing stairs is harder than writing)
  • Respiratory: Dyspnea, weak cough — can progress to respiratory failure

Treatment: Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) — acetylcholinesterase inhibitor; take BEFORE meals to help with chewing/swallowing. Also: immunosuppressants, plasmapheresis, IVIG, thymectomy.

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) - Deep Dive

🧠 Guillain-Barré Syndrome

Pathophysiology: Autoimmune demyelination of peripheral nerves (Schwann cells). Often follows a viral illness, GI infection (Campylobacter jejuni is classic), or vaccination by 1-3 weeks. The immune system attacks myelin — slows/blocks nerve conduction.

Hallmark feature: Ascending, symmetric paralysis — starts in legs and moves up. Often begins with tingling/numbness in feet.

Progression:

  1. Initial: Paresthesias in toes/feet; weakness begins
  2. Progresses upward over hours to days — legs → trunk → arms → face
  3. Peak danger: respiratory muscle involvement (monitor vital capacity and negative inspiratory force!)
  4. Autonomic instability (BP swings, arrhythmias) possible
  5. Plateau phase (days to weeks), then gradual descending recovery over weeks to months
⚠️ Top GBS Nursing Priority = AIRWAY

Respiratory failure is the leading cause of death. Assess respiratory effort every 2-4 hours:

  • Vital capacity < 15-20 mL/kg = concern for respiratory failure
  • Inability to count to 20 in one breath
  • Keep intubation equipment at bedside

Treatment: IVIG or plasmapheresis (NOT corticosteroids — don't help in GBS). Supportive care is key. DVT prophylaxis, skin care, passive ROM, emotional support (patients are often fully conscious but paralyzed — can hear everything).

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS) - Deep Dive

🧠 ALS ("Lou Gehrig's Disease")

Pathophysiology: Progressive degeneration of BOTH upper motor neurons (cortex, corticospinal tracts) AND lower motor neurons (anterior horn of spinal cord). No known cause in most cases. Average life expectancy: 3-5 years post-diagnosis (though some live much longer, like Stephen Hawking).

What is SPARED:

  • Sensation (patients feel everything normally)
  • Cognition (in most patients — they remain mentally intact)
  • Bladder/bowel control (usually preserved)
  • Eye movements (until very late stages)

What is LOST — progressive muscle atrophy:

  • Fasciculations (muscle twitches), cramping
  • Progressive weakness — usually asymmetric at start
  • Atrophy, spasticity, hyperreflexia
  • Dysphagia, dysarthria (bulbar symptoms)
  • Respiratory failure — ultimately the cause of death
🎯 ALS Nursing Priorities
  • Communication: Patient remains cognitively intact — provide alternative communication (letter boards, eye-gaze devices) as speech declines
  • Respiratory: Discuss BiPAP, tracheostomy, and ventilator decisions EARLY while patient can participate
  • Nutrition: Aspiration risk with dysphagia; may need PEG tube
  • Advance directives: End-of-life planning is essential
  • Medication: Riluzole may slow progression; Edaravone may help some patients

Autonomic Dysreflexia - Deep Dive

⚠️ Autonomic Dysreflexia - LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY

Who's at risk: Spinal cord injury at or above T6. Why T6? Splanchnic nerves exit at/below T6. Injury above this level means the brain can no longer regulate sympathetic output below the lesion.

Pathophysiology (step-by-step):

  1. A noxious stimulus occurs BELOW the level of injury (most often full bladder or impacted bowel)
  2. Signal travels up spinal cord but gets BLOCKED at the injury
  3. Unopposed massive sympathetic response below the injury → severe vasoconstriction → HYPERTENSION
  4. Above the injury: parasympathetic tries to compensate → vasodilation, flushing, headache, bradycardia
  5. BP can skyrocket (systolic 200-300 mmHg!) → stroke, seizure, death if not treated

Classic signs:

Above the InjuryBelow the Injury
Pounding headacheCool, pale, goosebumps
Flushed face/neck, sweatingVasoconstriction
Nasal congestion 
Blurred vision 
Bradycardia 
Severe HTN 

Nursing actions — DO THESE IN ORDER:

  1. SIT THE PATIENT UP immediately (to lower BP via orthostasis)
  2. Loosen tight clothing/restrictive devices
  3. Find and remove the trigger (in this order):
    • Check for distended bladder → catheterize if needed (use lidocaine jelly to avoid triggering more stimulation)
    • Check for bowel impaction → digital disimpaction (also with lidocaine)
    • Check skin for pressure injury, tight clothing, ingrown nail
  4. Monitor BP every 2-5 minutes
  5. Antihypertensives (nitroglycerin, hydralazine, nifedipine) if BP stays high after trigger removal
  6. Notify provider

Common triggers — patient education: Full bladder (most common!), constipation, UTI, pressure ulcers, tight clothing, ingrown toenail, sexual activity, labor. Patients and caregivers need to know these!

Seizures & Status Epilepticus - Deep Dive

🧠 Seizures

Pathophysiology: Abnormal, excessive electrical discharges from neurons. Can be focal (one area) or generalized (both hemispheres).

Types:

  • Tonic-clonic (grand mal): Loss of consciousness, tonic (stiff) phase → clonic (jerking) phase; postictal confusion follows
  • Absence (petit mal): Brief staring spells, 5-30 seconds, no postictal state — common in children
  • Focal aware: No LOC, one body area affected
  • Focal impaired awareness: Altered awareness, automatisms (lip-smacking, picking)

During a seizure — what to DO:

  • Protect head, clear area of hazards, loosen clothing
  • Turn patient on their SIDE (prevents aspiration)
  • Time the seizure
  • Do NOT restrain
  • Do NOT put anything in the mouth
  • Stay with patient
⚠️ Status Epilepticus

Definition: Seizure lasting >5 minutes OR recurrent seizures without return to baseline between them.

Why dangerous: Risk of permanent brain damage from sustained neuronal firing, hypoxia, and hyperthermia. Mortality is significant.

Treatment (in order):

  1. ABCs — airway, oxygen, IV access, monitor
  2. Lorazepam (Ativan) or diazepam IV — FIRST LINE
  3. Phenytoin or fosphenytoin IV if still seizing
  4. If refractory → phenobarbital, then general anesthesia (propofol, midazolam drip)

Phenytoin (Dilantin) teaching: Therapeutic level 10-20 mcg/mL. Causes gingival hyperplasia — emphasize oral hygiene and regular dental visits. Give through separate IV line (incompatible with many); flush with saline only (never dextrose — causes precipitation).

The Hearing Pathway

  1. Sound waves enter external auditory canal
  2. Tympanic membrane vibrates
  3. Ossicles amplify (malleus → incus → stapes)
  4. Stapes moves oval window; creates waves in cochlea
  5. Hair cells in organ of Corti bend
  6. Electrical impulses generated
  7. Impulses travel via CN VIII to temporal lobe

🎯 NCLEX: Weber vs Rinne

Weber: Tuning fork on forehead. Lateralizes to affected ear = Conductive. Lateralizes to unaffected ear = Sensorineural.

Rinne: AC vs BC. Normal: AC > BC. Conductive: BC > AC.

⚠️ Ménière's Disease - Deep Dive

Pathophysiology: Endolymphatic hydrops — excessive buildup of endolymph fluid in the inner ear causes increased pressure and distension of the membranous labyrinth. The exact cause is unknown, though may involve autoimmune or viral factors.

Classic triad — all three must usually be present:

  • Vertigo: Severe, episodic spinning sensation lasting minutes to hours. Often unpredictable and disabling. Not constant — comes in "attacks."
  • Tinnitus: Ringing, roaring, or buzzing in the affected ear. Often changes in character before an attack.
  • Fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss: Low frequencies affected first. May improve between attacks early on; becomes permanent over time.

Fourth symptom (often listed): Aural fullness — sensation of pressure in the ear.

🎯 Ménière's NCLEX Teaching
  • Low-sodium diet (2000 mg/day) — reduces endolymph production
  • Avoid caffeine, alcohol, nicotine (all trigger attacks)
  • Stress management
  • Safety during attacks: lie still in quiet, darkened room; no driving; fall precautions
  • Medications: Meclizine (antivert) for vertigo; diuretics (HCTZ) to reduce endolymph; antiemetics for nausea
  • Surgical option for severe cases: endolymphatic sac decompression or labyrinthectomy (last resort — destroys hearing in that ear)

Glaucoma - Deep Dive

👁️ Glaucoma

Pathophysiology: Increased intraocular pressure (IOP) damages the optic nerve → progressive, irreversible vision loss. Normal IOP is 10-21 mmHg. The leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide.

  • Prostaglandin analogs (latanoprost): First-line. May darken iris color, lengthen eyelashes. Use at bedtime.
  • Beta-blockers (timolol): Apply nasolacrimal occlusion (press inner corner of eye for 1 min after instilling) to prevent systemic absorption. Use cautiously in asthma, heart block.
  • Miotics (pilocarpine): Constrict pupil to open angle. Cause dim vision, brow ache.
  • Never use mydriatics/atropine in angle-closure glaucoma — will precipitate an attack!
  • Drop technique: wash hands, tilt head back, pull lower lid down, don't touch dropper to eye, close eye gently, wait 5 min between different drops

Retinal Detachment - Deep Dive

⚠️ Retinal Detachment - VISION EMERGENCY

Pathophysiology: The sensory retina separates from the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) beneath it. Without the RPE's blood supply, photoreceptors die within hours — time-critical. The longer detached, the less vision returns even after repair.

Three types:

  • Rhegmatogenous (most common): A tear/hole lets fluid seep behind the retina. Associated with myopia, aging, trauma.
  • Tractional: Scar tissue pulls the retina away. Common in diabetic retinopathy.
  • Exudative: Fluid accumulates beneath retina without tear. Causes: tumors, inflammation.

Classic symptoms (painless!):

  • Flashes (photopsia): Brief flashes of light in peripheral vision — "like lightning"
  • Floaters: Sudden onset of many new floaters ("cobwebs," "black spots," "hairs")
  • Curtain or shadow: Progressive loss of visual field — "curtain coming down"
  • Blurred vision if macula involved

Nursing priorities:

  1. Position the patient so gravity keeps retina in contact with RPE (depends on location of tear — provider will specify)
  2. Restrict activity, bed rest, avoid Valsalva
  3. Prepare for emergent surgery (vitrectomy, scleral buckle, pneumatic retinopexy, or laser)
  4. Address anxiety — sudden vision loss is terrifying

Post-op teaching: If a gas bubble was placed, strict positioning (often face-down for days); no air travel until bubble resolves (altitude change = bubble expansion = dangerous IOP spike); avoid bending, lifting >10 lbs, strenuous activity.

Macular Degeneration - Deep Dive

👁️ Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

Pathophysiology: Deterioration of the macula — the small central area of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed central vision (reading, faces, fine detail). Peripheral vision is preserved. Leading cause of vision loss in adults over 60.

Type% of CasesProgressionTreatment
Dry (atrophic)~85-90%Slow; drusen (yellow deposits) accumulate; gradual central vision blurAREDS2 vitamins (C, E, zinc, copper, lutein, zeaxanthin) — slow progression but don't reverse; smoking cessation; UV protection

Key patient teaching — Amsler grid: Home monitoring tool. A grid that should look square and straight. If lines appear wavy, distorted, or missing → call provider immediately (sign of progression, especially wet AMD).

Safety adaptations: Bright lighting, large-print materials, magnifiers, talking books, contrast-enhanced kitchen tools, fall precautions, smoke cessation (smoking doubles AMD risk).

Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis) - Deep Dive

⚠️ Thyroid Storm - LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY

What it is: Severe, life-threatening exacerbation of hyperthyroidism. Mortality 20-30% even with treatment. Usually occurs in patients with undiagnosed or poorly controlled Graves' disease who experience a trigger.

Common triggers: Infection, surgery, trauma, DKA, pregnancy, abrupt withdrawal of antithyroid medication, iodine load (contrast dye).

Signs & symptoms (think: EVERYTHING revved up to dangerous levels):

  • Extreme hyperthermia: temperature up to 105-106°F (40-41°C)
  • Tachycardia: HR often >140, atrial fibrillation, high-output heart failure
  • Severe HTN initially, then hypotension as decompensation sets in
  • CNS: Agitation, delirium, psychosis, seizures, coma
  • GI: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, jaundice
  • Severe diaphoresis, dehydration
🎯 Thyroid Storm Treatment Order
  1. Block hormone synthesis: PTU (propylthiouracil) OR methimazole
  2. Block hormone release: Iodine (SSKI, Lugol's) — given >1 hour AFTER PTU (otherwise feeds thyroid)
  3. Block peripheral effects: Propranolol (IV for severe tachycardia, HTN, tremors)
  4. Block T4→T3 conversion: Hydrocortisone or dexamethasone
  5. Supportive: Cooling blankets, acetaminophen (NOT aspirin — displaces thyroid hormone from proteins!), IV fluids, treat trigger

Myxedema Coma - Deep Dive

⚠️ Myxedema Coma - LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY

What it is: Severe, decompensated hypothyroidism. Mortality 30-60% even with treatment. Usually occurs in elderly women with longstanding, undertreated hypothyroidism who experience a trigger.

Common triggers: Cold exposure (winter months are peak), infection, surgery, trauma, sedatives, opioids, sudden discontinuation of levothyroxine.

Signs & symptoms (think: EVERYTHING shut down):

  • Hypothermia: temperature often <95°F (35°C) — may be why it's missed
  • Bradycardia: HR 40-50 range
  • Hypotension
  • Hypoventilation: Shallow, slow respirations → CO2 retention → worsening mental status
  • Altered mental status: Confusion → lethargy → stupor → coma
  • Hyponatremia (from impaired free water excretion)
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Myxedema: Non-pitting, doughy edema (especially periorbital, hands, feet)
🎯 Myxedema Coma Treatment
  1. Airway support: Often requires intubation
  2. IV levothyroxine (T4) — cautious loading dose; some add T3
  3. IV hydrocortisone (adrenal insufficiency often coexists — give BEFORE thyroid hormone)
  4. Gradual rewarming with blankets (not active warming — can cause vasodilation and shock)
  5. IV fluids: Normal saline (hypotonic may worsen hyponatremia)
  6. Treat underlying trigger (often infection — get cultures, start empiric antibiotics)

Cushing Syndrome - Deep Dive

🏔️ Cushing Syndrome

Pathophysiology: Prolonged exposure to excess cortisol. Since cortisol is catabolic and has widespread metabolic effects, symptoms hit nearly every body system.

Causes (differentiate!):

  • Exogenous (most common): Long-term corticosteroid use (prednisone for asthma, RA, lupus, transplant)
  • Cushing DISEASE: Pituitary adenoma producing excess ACTH (specific subtype)
  • Adrenal tumor: Directly secretes cortisol
  • Ectopic ACTH: Non-pituitary tumor (often lung cancer) secreting ACTH

Classic appearance changes:

  • Moon face (rounded, red cheeks)
  • Buffalo hump (fat pad on upper back)
  • Truncal/central obesity with thin extremities (muscle wasting)
  • Purple striae on abdomen, thighs, breasts (skin thinning + rapid weight gain)
  • Thin, fragile skin; easy bruising; poor wound healing
  • Hirsutism (facial hair in women), acne
  • Menstrual irregularities, decreased libido

Lab findings — MEMORIZE:

LabDirectionWhy
Glucose↑ HIGHCortisol antagonizes insulin, stimulates gluconeogenesis
Sodium↑ HIGHCortisol has some mineralocorticoid activity
Potassium↓ LOWRenal K+ excretion increased

🎯 Cushing Nursing Priorities
  • Infection prevention: Cortisol suppresses immunity — reverse isolation if severe; meticulous skin care; monitor for subtle signs (fever may be blunted!)
  • Skin safety: Fragile skin — use pressure-reducing surfaces, turn q2h, paper tape
  • Blood glucose monitoring — may need insulin
  • Diet: Low sodium, low calorie, high protein, high calcium/vitamin D, high potassium
  • Emotional support: Body image distress is significant; mood changes from cortisol itself
  • If due to exogenous steroids: NEVER stop abruptly — taper slowly to avoid Addisonian crisis

Addison Disease - Deep Dive

🏔️ Addison Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)

Pathophysiology: The adrenal cortex is destroyed/damaged → insufficient production of BOTH cortisol AND aldosterone. Most common cause in developed countries is autoimmune destruction (80%). Other causes: TB, metastatic cancer, hemorrhage, HIV.

Signs & symptoms (think: EVERYTHING drops):

  • Hyperpigmentation (bronze skin — especially creases, scars, gums, nipples, buccal mucosa) — happens because low cortisol → high ACTH, and ACTH stimulates melanin. This is unique to PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency!
  • Fatigue, weakness, muscle aches
  • Weight loss, anorexia
  • Hypotension (worse when standing — orthostatic)
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain
  • Salt craving (from aldosterone deficiency)
  • Decreased libido, amenorrhea
  • Depression, irritability

Lab findings — MIRROR IMAGE of Cushing:

LabDirectionWhy

⚠️ Addisonian Crisis (Acute Adrenal Crisis) - LIFE-THREATENING

What it is: Sudden, severe drop in cortisol/aldosterone causing circulatory collapse. Without treatment: death in hours.

Common triggers:

  • Abrupt steroid withdrawal (biggest preventable cause — why patients NEVER stop steroids suddenly)
  • Stress in an Addison patient without stress-dose steroids (surgery, trauma, infection, labor)
  • Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage

Signs: Severe hypotension/shock, altered mental status, severe abdominal/flank/leg pain ("Addisonian" pain), hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, fever, vomiting, dehydration.

🎯 Addisonian Crisis Treatment
  1. IV hydrocortisone (Solu-Cortef) STAT — 100 mg IV push, then infusion
  2. IV fluids: Normal saline (often multiple liters for volume resuscitation)
  3. Dextrose for hypoglycemia
  4. Correct electrolytes (hyperkalemia, hyponatremia)
  5. Treat underlying trigger (infection, etc.)
  6. Continuous cardiac monitoring
🚨 Patient education to PREVENT crisis:
  • Medical alert bracelet/ID stating adrenal insufficiency
  • Never miss a dose of oral steroids; carry backup
  • Stress-dose sick-day rules: Double or triple oral dose during minor illness (fever, vomiting, dental work)
  • Home IM hydrocortisone kit (Solu-Cortef Act-O-Vial) for emergencies when they can't keep oral meds down
  • Seek emergency care for vomiting, severe illness, trauma, surgery
  • Inform ALL providers (dentist, surgeon) about diagnosis
