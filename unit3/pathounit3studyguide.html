🧠 Chapter 14: Nervous System Disorders

Neuron
Nerve cell; fundamental unit composed of cell body, axon, and dendrites
Cell Body (Soma)
Contains nucleus and organelles; supports metabolic needs of the cell
Dendrite
Multiple, branched extensions that TRANSMIT impulses TO the cell body
Axon
Carries impulses AWAY from the cell body
Sensory Neurons (Afferent)
Carry impulses from receptors in periphery TO the CNS
Motor Neurons (Efferent)
Carry signals AWAY from brain/spinal cord to regulate activity
Interneurons
Most abundant; provide connections between afferent and efferent neurons
Neuroglia
Neural support cells; provide nutrition, form myelin, maintain homeostasis
Myelin
Insulating protein high in lipid; increases speed of nerve impulses
Action Potential
Electrical signal; 3 phases: Resting → Depolarization (Na+ in) → Repolarization (K+ out)
Synapse
Junction between neurons; electrical (fast, gap junctions) or chemical (neurotransmitters)
Dermatome
Sensory region innervated by single pair of dorsal root ganglia
Serotonin
Neurotransmitter involved in mood, emotions, and sleep
Local (Focal) Effects
Signs related to specific area of brain/spinal cord where lesion is located

Central Nervous System (CNS)

Composed of the brain and spinal cord

  • Brain: Receives and processes sensory information, initiates motor responses
  • Spinal Cord: Conducts sensory information from PNS and motor information to muscles

Action Potentials - How Neurons Communicate

Neurons communicate via electrical events across cell membranes:

  1. Resting Membrane Potential (RMP): Cell at rest; Na+/K+ ATPase maintains balance
  2. Depolarization Phase: Rapid movement of Na+ INTO the cell through sodium channels; triggers release of neurotransmitters
  3. Repolarization Phase: K+ flows OUT of the cell; returns to RMP

⭐ MULTIPLE SCLEROSIS (MS) - Teacher's #1 Focus!

Pathophysiology

Autoimmune disorder causing demyelination of CNS neurons. Myelin is destroyed, forming scar tissue (sclerosis) that slows/blocks nerve impulses.

Classic Signs & Symptoms

👁️ Visual changes (optic neuritis, diplopia)
💪 Motor weakness, spasticity
🤚 Sensory changes (numbness, tingling)
🚶 Coordination problems (ataxia)
😴 Profound fatigue (worse with heat)
🧠 Cognitive changes
🚽 Bladder/bowel dysfunction

Key Concepts for NCLEX

🌡️
Uhthoff's Phenomenon: MS symptoms temporarily worsen with increased body temperature (hot baths, exercise, fever). Avoid heat exposure!
⚡
Lhermitte's Sign: Electric shock sensation down spine when bending neck forward - classic MS finding!

Nursing Priorities

  • Prevent injury (fall precautions, vision aids)
  • Manage fatigue (energy conservation, rest periods)
  • Promote mobility (PT, assistive devices)
  • Address bladder/bowel function
  • Patient education about heat avoidance
  • Medication adherence (immunomodulators)

Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA)

Temporary localized reduction of blood flow in brain; recovery usually within 24 hours. May occur as single event or in series. WARNING SIGN for stroke!

Cerebrovascular Accident (CVA/Stroke)

Infarction of brain tissue from lack of blood. Tissue necrosis may be from:

  • Total occlusion by atheroma or embolus → ischemia
  • Ruptured cerebral vessel → hemorrhage and increased ICP

Parkinson's Disease

Pathophysiology

Degeneration of dopamine-producing neurons in substantia nigra → dopamine deficiency

TRAP: Tremor, Rigidity, Akinesia (bradykinesia), Postural instability

Myasthenia Gravis

Antibodies block acetylcholine receptors at neuromuscular junction

Key Feature: Weakness worsens with activity, improves with rest!

Signs: Ptosis, diplopia, dysphagia, respiratory weakness

Guillain-Barré Syndrome

Autoimmune demyelination of peripheral nerves (PNS)

Ascending paralysis - starts in legs, moves UP!

Danger: Respiratory compromise - monitor closely!

Control Center

The hypothalamic-pituitary axis controls synthesis and secretion of many hormones.

Hypothalamus - Releasing Hormones

  • GHRH: Growth hormone-releasing hormone
  • TRH: Thyrotropin-releasing hormone
  • CRH: Corticotropin-releasing hormone
  • GnRH: Gonadotropin-releasing hormone

  • Somatostatin: Inhibits GH and TSH
  • Dopamine: Inhibits prolactin

🤱 OXYTOCIN - Teacher's MUST KNOW!

SourceTargetFunction
Hypothalamus → Posterior Pituitary (stored/released)Uterus and BreastsUterus: Stimulates contractions during labor
Breasts: Milk ejection after childbirth

Key NCLEX Points:

  • Also called the "love hormone" - involved in bonding
  • Used to induce/augment labor
  • NCLEX Alert: Monitor for water intoxication (ADH-like effect at high doses)
  • Triggers let-down reflex for breastfeeding

Posterior Pituitary Hormones

ADH and oxytocin travel along nerve axons to posterior pituitary for unchanged release into systemic circulation.

Anterior Pituitary Actions

  1. Hormone travels to APG → released as-is (ex: Prolactin)
  2. Releasing hormone acts on APG → APG produces different hormone (ex: Growth Hormone)
  3. Releasing hormone → APG releases stimulating hormone → acts on gland to produce another hormone (ex: Thyroid Hormone)

Negative Feedback (Most Common)

Like a thermostat - when levels are high, production turns off; when low, production turns on.

Examples:

  • Aldosterone levels adjust based on Na+ and K+ levels
  • Thyroid hormone (T3, T4) levels alert hypothalamus and anterior pituitary to adjust TRH and TSH secretion

Positive Feedback (Less Common)

Hormone stimulates increased production of the same hormone.

Example: Dilation/stretch of cervix triggers oxytocin release → promotes continued stretch + contractions → increases oxytocin release until birth

Hormonal Response to Stress

Stress → CRH from hypothalamus → ACTH from pituitary → Cortisol from adrenal cortex

Cortisol Effects: Increases metabolism, regulates blood glucose, anti-inflammatory

General Adaptation Syndrome - 3 Stages

1. Alarm Stage (Fight or Flight)

Catecholamines and cortisol released; other hormones decrease (reproductive, TH, GH) or increase (ADH); hormone levels return to baseline if stressor removed

2. Resistance Stage

Persistent stress → cortisol levels decrease through negative feedback; hypercortisolism is detrimental: exhaustion of inflammatory/immune responses, tissue breakdown, glucose intolerance

3. Exhaustion Stage

Chronic overwhelming stress → energy depletion, degeneration of cells, tissues, organs, organ systems
