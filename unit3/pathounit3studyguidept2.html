🎯 High-Yield Exam Focus

GERD Management - HIGH YIELD

🔥 GERD Teaching Points

  • Wait 2-3 hours after eating before lying down
  • Eat 4-6 small meals instead of 3 large
  • Avoid triggers (spicy, fatty, caffeine, alcohol)
  • Elevate head of bed 6-8 inches
  • Sleep on LEFT side to reduce reflux

Cirrhosis & Lactulose

🫘 Why Lactulose?

Cirrhosis impairs ammonia → urea conversion. Lactulose traps ammonia in gut as ammonium; eliminated via stool.

NCLEX Alert: If patient says "I'm not constipated" — educate that it's for AMMONIA levels, not just constipation!

Signs of Advanced Cirrhosis: Petechiae, ascites, jaundice, hepatic encephalopathy, portal hypertension

🚨 Small Bowel Obstruction

Withhold: Laxatives (Senna), stimulants — can worsen obstruction!

Paralytic Ileus Prevention: Increase ambulation post-op to promote peristalsis

CKD Monitoring

🫘 Best Measure of Fluid Retention

DAILY WEIGHT — most reliable (1 kg = 1 L fluid)

  • Hyperkalemia (dangerous!)
  • Metabolic acidosis

⚠️ NCLEX Alert: Aspiration Risk

Position patient on SIDE during vomiting. Patients with decreased LOC cannot protect airway. Aspiration pneumonia = serious complication.

Steatorrhea: Fat Malabsorption

"Fatty diarrhea" — frequent, bulky, greasy, foul-smelling stools; floats in toilet.

Causes: Pancreatic insufficiency, bile deficiency, mucosal disease (celiac, Crohn's), lymphatic obstruction

⚠️ Celiac Disease - HIGH YIELD

Pathophysiology: Autoimmune damage to villi triggered by gluten (wheat, barley, rye).

Associated Conditions: Type 1 DM, autoimmune thyroid, dermatitis herpetiformis

Classic Presentation: Chronic diarrhea, steatorrhea, abdominal bloating, weight loss, iron deficiency anemia (refractory to oral iron)

Diagnostic: tTG-IgA antibody → confirmed by small intestinal biopsy. Patient MUST be eating gluten for accurate testing!

Treatment: LIFELONG gluten-free diet (avoid WBR — wheat, barley, rye)

Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD) - Deep Dive

🍽️ PUD: Gastric vs Duodenal Ulcers

Pathophysiology: Imbalance between aggressive factors (acid, pepsin, H. pylori, NSAIDs, alcohol, stress) and protective factors (mucus layer, bicarbonate, prostaglandins, blood flow) → mucosal erosion through the muscularis mucosa.

FeatureGastric UlcerDuodenal Ulcer
LocationStomach (lesser curvature most common)First 1-2 cm of duodenum
Pain timing30 min - 1 hr AFTER meals; WORSE with food2-3 hrs after meals; BETTER with food
Nocturnal painLess commonCommon ("wakes patient at night")
WeightLoss (afraid to eat)Often maintained or gained
H. pylori association~70%~90%
Cancer riskPossible — biopsy every gastric ulcer!Rarely malignant
Bleeding presentationHematemesis more commonMelena more common
🎯 PUD Medications & H. pylori Eradication
  • PPIs (omeprazole, pantoprazole): Block H+/K+ ATPase; take before breakfast; most effective acid suppressors; long-term risks include C. diff, B12 deficiency, osteoporosis
  • H2 blockers (famotidine, ranitidine): Less potent; can be used at bedtime for nocturnal acid
  • Sucralfate (Carafate): Coats ulcer — take on EMPTY stomach, 1 hour before meals; may bind other meds
  • Antacids: Symptomatic relief only; don't heal ulcers alone
  • H. pylori "triple therapy": PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin (or metronidazole if PCN allergy) × 10-14 days
  • Avoid: NSAIDs, aspirin, alcohol, smoking, caffeine during healing
⚠️ PUD Complications - CRITICAL EMERGENCIES

1. Hemorrhage (most common complication):

  • Signs: Hematemesis ("coffee grounds" or bright red), melena (black tarry stool), hypotension, tachycardia, decreased Hgb/Hct
  • Action: NPO, large-bore IV × 2, type and cross, PPI infusion, endoscopic intervention (cautery, clipping, banding)

2. Perforation (most dangerous):

  • Signs: Sudden, severe, generalized abdominal pain ("worst pain of my life"); rigid "board-like" abdomen; rebound tenderness; absent bowel sounds; shoulder pain (referred from diaphragm irritation); shock
  • Upright CXR shows free air under diaphragm
  • Action: EMERGENT SURGERY! NPO, NG tube to suction, IV fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, pain control

3. Gastric outlet obstruction:

  • Signs: Early satiety, vomiting undigested food, weight loss, succussion splash
  • Action: NG decompression, IV fluids, PPI, endoscopic dilation or surgery

Cirrhosis - Deep Dive

🍷 Cirrhosis of the Liver

Pathophysiology: Chronic, progressive liver disease characterized by diffuse fibrosis and nodular regeneration. Hepatocytes die faster than they can regenerate → scar tissue replaces functional liver → impaired blood flow (portal hypertension) + impaired function (everything the liver does).

Common causes:

  • Alcohol abuse (most common in US)
  • Chronic viral hepatitis (B and C)
  • Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH — associated with obesity, diabetes)
  • Autoimmune hepatitis, biliary disorders
  • Hemochromatosis, Wilson's disease

Why each manifestation happens:

SignMechanism
AscitesPortal HTN + hypoalbuminemia (liver can't make albumin) + sodium/water retention (low albumin → aldosterone activation)
Esophageal/gastric varicesPortal HTN forces blood through collateral veins; these dilate and can rupture catastrophically
SplenomegalyPortal HTN backs blood into spleen → thrombocytopenia (spleen sequesters platelets)
JaundiceLiver can't conjugate/excrete bilirubin
Coagulopathy (↑PT/INR)Liver makes clotting factors II, VII, IX, X; also vitamin K malabsorption
Hepatic encephalopathyAmmonia (from protein metabolism) can't be converted to urea → crosses BBB → confusion, asterixis, coma
Gynecomastia, palmar erythema, spider angiomasLiver can't metabolize estrogen → relative estrogen excess in men
Fetor hepaticusSweet, musty breath odor from unmetabolized mercaptans
Caput medusaeVisible dilated periumbilical veins from portal HTN
⚠️ Hepatic Encephalopathy Stages
StageFindings
1Subtle personality changes, sleep disturbance, mild confusion
2Lethargy, asterixis ("liver flap" — flapping hand tremor when wrists extended), disorientation to time
3Marked confusion, stupor but arousable, slurred speech
4Coma, unresponsive

Precipitants: GI bleed (blood in gut = protein load = more ammonia!), infection, constipation, high-protein meal, dehydration, certain medications (sedatives, diuretics)

🎯 Cirrhosis Nursing Priorities
  • Lactulose: Target 2-3 soft stools/day (acidifies gut, traps NH3 as NH4+). Don't worry about the diarrhea — it's working!
  • Rifaximin: Non-absorbed antibiotic — reduces ammonia-producing gut bacteria; often added to lactulose
  • Diet: Moderate protein (too low worsens muscle wasting, too high worsens encephalopathy); low sodium (≤2g) for ascites; fluid restriction if hyponatremic; small frequent meals
  • Varices precautions: Soft foods, no straws, no NG tubes if possible (can rupture varices); beta-blockers (propranolol) for prevention
  • Variceal bleeding: Octreotide infusion (lowers portal pressure), endoscopic banding/sclerotherapy, balloon tamponade (Sengstaken-Blakemore) as last resort
  • Ascites management: Spironolactone (first-line diuretic — liver makes less SHBG, more aldosterone), furosemide; paracentesis for respiratory compromise (monitor for shock, albumin replacement)
  • Bleeding precautions: Soft toothbrush, electric razor, no IM injections, pressure after sticks, fall prevention
  • Avoid: Acetaminophen >2g/day, NSAIDs, alcohol, sedatives (can precipitate encephalopathy)

Bowel Obstruction - Deep Dive

⚠️ Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO) vs Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)
FeatureSBOLBO
Common causesAdhesions (#1 — from prior surgery), hernia, tumor, volvulus, intussusceptionColorectal cancer, volvulus, diverticulitis stricture, fecal impaction
VomitingEARLY, frequent, can be biliousLATE, feculent (ominous!)
Abdominal painColicky, cramping, intermittent (every 3-10 min)Deeper, more continuous
DistensionMild to moderateMarked
Bowel soundsHigh-pitched "tinkling" or hyperactive EARLY → absent LATEHypoactive from start
Dehydration/electrolytesRapid fluid loss; metabolic alkalosis (from vomiting HCl)Slower; metabolic acidosis possible

Imaging: Abdominal X-ray shows dilated loops, air-fluid levels (ladder pattern in SBO). CT is most accurate and identifies cause.

Partial vs Complete obstruction:

  • Partial: Some gas/stool still passes; may resolve with NG decompression and NPO
  • Complete: No passage; higher risk of strangulation/perforation → often surgery

Strangulation = surgical emergency! Signs: fever, tachycardia, severe unrelenting pain, peritoneal signs, rising lactate, leukocytosis. Risk of perforation → sepsis → death.

🎯 SBO Nursing Priorities
  1. NPO — nothing by mouth!
  2. NG tube to low intermittent suction — decompress stomach/bowel, measure output, replace losses
  3. IV fluids (0.9% NS usually) — aggressive rehydration
  4. Electrolyte correction — especially K+ (lost in vomiting/NG drainage)
  5. Strict I&O, daily weights
  6. WITHHOLD laxatives! Stimulating peristalsis against obstruction = perforation risk
  7. Pain management (opioids cautiously — can worsen ileus)
  8. Monitor for perforation: Sudden pain relief (possible rupture), fever, rigid abdomen, tachycardia, hypotension
  9. Surgical consult if no improvement in 24-48 hours or signs of strangulation/perforation

Paralytic Ileus - Deep Dive

🍽️ Paralytic Ileus (Adynamic Ileus)

Pathophysiology: Temporary cessation of peristalsis — there's no mechanical blockage, but the gut isn't moving. Essentially "bowel stunned" into silence.

Common causes:

  • Post-op (very common after abdominal surgery — normal up to 3-5 days)
  • Opioids (especially for chronic use)
  • Electrolyte imbalances (HYPOKALEMIA is a big one!)
  • Peritonitis, pancreatitis, sepsis
  • Spinal cord injury, immobility
  • Anticholinergic medications

Signs: Distended abdomen, NO or very hypoactive bowel sounds, no passage of flatus/stool, vague discomfort (rarely colicky like SBO), nausea/vomiting

🎯 Prevention & Treatment
  • Prevention is KEY — especially post-op:
    • Early ambulation (up in chair within hours, walking ASAP)
    • Adequate hydration
    • Correct electrolytes (check K+!)
    • Minimize opioids — use multimodal pain control
    • Gum chewing post-op (shown to reduce ileus)
  • Treatment: NPO, NG decompression if severe, IV fluids, correct electrolytes, treat underlying cause, withhold opioids if possible
  • NCLEX tip: If a post-op patient has no bowel sounds, no flatus, and abdominal distension, the priority is usually ambulation — NOT a laxative!

Pyelonephritis - Deep Dive

⚠️ Pyelonephritis - Upper UTI Emergency Potential

Pathophysiology: Bacterial infection of the renal pelvis and parenchyma, usually ascending from a lower UTI. Most common pathogen: E. coli (~75-90%). Can cause permanent kidney scarring, sepsis, and death.

Risk factors: Female anatomy, pregnancy, diabetes, urinary obstruction, catheters, VUR (reflux), immunosuppression

Classic presentation:

  • Triad: Fever + flank pain + CVA tenderness
  • All cystitis symptoms (dysuria, frequency, urgency)
  • Systemic: chills, malaise, anorexia, nausea, vomiting
  • Costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness on percussion

Lab findings:

  • Urinalysis: pyuria, bacteriuria, leukocyte esterase positive, nitrite positive, WBC casts (distinguishes upper from lower UTI!)
  • CBC: leukocytosis with left shift
  • Blood cultures (20-30% have bacteremia)
🎯 Pyelonephritis Treatment
  • Outpatient (mild): Oral fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin) × 7 days — but ONLY if not pregnant, not septic, can tolerate oral
  • Inpatient (moderate-severe, pregnant, vomiting, septic): IV ceftriaxone or fluoroquinolone × 10-14 days
  • Complications to monitor for: Renal abscess, sepsis, emphysematous pyelonephritis (in diabetics), chronic pyelonephritis with renal scarring
  • Teaching: Complete full antibiotic course even if feeling better, increase fluids, follow-up urine culture, prevent recurrence

AKI - Deep Dive

🫘 Acute Kidney Injury

Definition: Sudden decrease in kidney function (hours to days). Measured by RISING creatinine or DROPPING urine output. Unlike CKD, AKI is often REVERSIBLE if caught early.

The 3 Categories — where's the problem?

TypeWhere the Problem IsCommon CausesLab Clues
Prerenal (55-60%)BEFORE the kidney — not enough blood getting TO itDehydration, hemorrhage, heart failure, sepsis, liver failure, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors (in volume-depleted patients)BUN:Cr >20:1, FeNa <1%, urine concentrated (high specific gravity), hyaline casts
Intrinsic (35-40%)WITHIN the kidney — tissue damageATN (ischemia or nephrotoxins like contrast dye, aminoglycosides, cisplatin), glomerulonephritis, interstitial nephritis (PPIs, NSAIDs, antibiotics), rhabdomyolysisBUN:Cr ~10-15:1, FeNa >2%, MUDDY BROWN CASTS (pathognomonic for ATN), RBC casts (glomerulonephritis), WBC casts (interstitial)
Postrenal (<5%)AFTER the kidney — outflow blockedBPH (most common), kidney stones, tumor, stricture, retention (neurogenic bladder)Variable; hydronephrosis on ultrasound; relieved by catheter or stent

The 4 Phases of AKI (know the timeline!):

  1. Onset/Initiation (hours-days): From insult to initial signs; reversible if caught
  2. Oliguric (1-3 weeks): Urine output <400 mL/day; this is when complications peak — hyperkalemia, fluid overload, acidosis, uremia
  3. Diuretic (1-2 weeks): Urine output rises (can exceed 3-5 L/day!); risk of dehydration and hypokalemia; BUN/Cr start improving
  4. Recovery (months): GFR gradually normalizes; some never fully recover
⚠️ Hyperkalemia in AKI — Deep Dive on Treatment

Why it's so dangerous: Potassium affects cardiac membrane potential. Progressive ECG changes:

  • K+ 5.5-6.5: Peaked, narrow T waves
  • K+ 6.5-7.5: Prolonged PR, flattened/absent P waves
  • K+ 7.5-8.5: Widened QRS
  • K+ >8.5: Sine wave pattern → V-fib → asystole → DEATH

🎯 Preventing AKI — Nursing Priorities
  • Avoid nephrotoxins: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides (when possible), IV contrast in high-risk patients
  • Pre-contrast hydration: 0.9% NS before/after IV contrast — IV hydration protects kidneys
  • Monitor urine output: Report <30 mL/hr or <0.5 mL/kg/hr
  • Daily weights, I&O, electrolytes, BUN/Cr
  • Dose-adjust medications for renal function
  • Aggressive sepsis management — sepsis is a top cause of AKI
Common Causes

  • Hypertension (2nd leading)
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease

Testicular Torsion - Deep Dive

⚠️ Testicular Torsion - UROLOGIC EMERGENCY!

Pathophysiology: The spermatic cord twists on itself → compromises blood supply (testicular artery) → testicular ischemia → infarction and death of the testicle. Commonly due to a "bell clapper deformity" (testis not properly anchored to tunica vaginalis), which allows free rotation.

Peak ages: Newborns AND adolescents 12-18 (most common). Can occur at any age.

Classic presentation:

  • Sudden, severe scrotal pain — often wakes patient from sleep or begins during/after activity
  • Nausea and vomiting (visceral response)
  • Affected testis rides HIGH in scrotum ("high-riding"), may be horizontal
  • Scrotal swelling, erythema
  • ABSENT cremasteric reflex on affected side (stroke inner thigh → testicle normally elevates; in torsion, it doesn't)
  • Negative Prehn sign (elevating testicle does NOT relieve pain — distinguishes from epididymitis)
🎯 The 6-Hour Window
Time from OnsetTesticle Salvage Rate
<6 hours~90-100%
6-12 hours~50%
12-24 hours~20%
>24 hours<10%

Treatment: EMERGENT surgical exploration (scrotal orchiopexy — untwist and suture BOTH testicles to prevent recurrence on other side). Manual detorsion may be attempted while preparing for surgery ("open the book" rotation — medial to lateral).

DO NOT: Delay for labs, imaging, pain control, or consultation. If the clinical picture is classic, go directly to surgery!

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) - Deep Dive

⚥ BPH

Pathophysiology: Age-related hyperplasia (INCREASE in NUMBER of cells, not size) of stromal and glandular tissue in the transitional zone of the prostate. Driven by dihydrotestosterone (DHT). By age 80, ~80% of men have BPH. It is NOT cancer and does NOT increase cancer risk.

Why it causes symptoms: Prostate surrounds the urethra → enlargement compresses urethra → outflow obstruction → bladder works harder → bladder muscle hypertrophies → can eventually fail.

Classic presentation — LUTS (Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms):

Complications: Acute urinary retention, recurrent UTIs, bladder stones, bladder decompensation, hydronephrosis, postrenal AKI

Diagnostic: DRE (smooth, enlarged prostate — vs. firm/nodular in cancer); PSA (may be mildly elevated); post-void residual >100 mL; urodynamic studies; ultrasound

⚠️ TURP (Transurethral Resection of Prostate) Post-Op

Standard surgical treatment for severe BPH.

Monitor for:

  • Hemorrhage: Continuous bladder irrigation (CBI) with NS — keep urine "light pink lemonade"; if bright red, increase rate; if clots, notify provider
  • TURP syndrome: Hyponatremia from absorption of irrigation fluid → confusion, nausea, seizures, bradycardia. Treat with hypertonic saline, diuretics.
  • Bladder spasms: Belladonna & opium suppositories, antispasmodics
  • Retrograde ejaculation (semen into bladder): Common post-TURP; not harmful but affects fertility — teach this pre-op!
  • Urinary incontinence: Usually temporary; Kegel exercises help

Teaching: No heavy lifting (>10 lbs), no driving, no strenuous activity × 4-6 weeks; no sex × 6 weeks; increase fluids; avoid constipation (straining can cause bleeding)

Prostatitis

⚥ Prostatitis

Acute bacterial prostatitis: Sudden fever, chills, pelvic/perineal pain, dysuria, cloudy urine, very tender boggy prostate on DRE

Treatment: Fluoroquinolones or TMP-SMX × 4-6 weeks (longer than UTI because prostate is hard to penetrate)

NCLEX Alert: NEVER vigorously massage an acutely inflamed prostate — can cause bacteremia/sepsis!

Endometriosis - Deep Dive

♀ Endometriosis

Pathophysiology: Endometrial-like tissue grows OUTSIDE the uterus — most commonly on ovaries (forming "chocolate cysts" — endometriomas filled with old blood), fallopian tubes, pelvic peritoneum, bladder, and bowel. This tissue still responds to the menstrual cycle: it proliferates with estrogen, sheds with menstruation, but has nowhere to drain → inflammation, scarring, adhesions.

Key theory: Retrograde menstruation (Sampson's theory) — some menstrual blood flows backward through fallopian tubes during periods, depositing endometrial cells.

Classic presentation (the "3 D's"):

  • Dysmenorrhea: Severe, cyclical pelvic pain starting 1-2 days BEFORE period and lasting into it; often progressive year over year
  • Dyspareunia: Deep pain with intercourse
  • Dyschezia: Pain with bowel movements (especially during menses)

Other signs: Infertility (30-50% of women with endometriosis), cyclic bladder/bowel symptoms, chronic pelvic pain, heavy/irregular menses, fatigue

Diagnostic:

  • Laparoscopy with biopsy = GOLD STANDARD — direct visualization of implants ("powder burn" spots, chocolate cysts, adhesions). Can also be therapeutic (ablation during same procedure).
  • Transvaginal ultrasound — limited; good for endometriomas >1 cm
  • CA-125 may be elevated but is NOT specific (elevated in ovarian cancer too)
  • Clinical diagnosis often made before laparoscopy and empiric treatment started

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - Deep Dive

⚠️ PID - Major Cause of Infertility

Pathophysiology: Ascending infection of the upper female reproductive tract (uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries, surrounding peritoneum). Most cases are caused by untreated or undertreated STIs — Chlamydia trachomatis (~50%) and Neisseria gonorrhoeae (~25%), often polymicrobial.

Progression:

  1. Cervicitis (lower tract infection)
  2. Endometritis (inflammation of uterine lining)
  3. Salpingitis (fallopian tube inflammation — where scarring/fertility damage occurs)
  4. Tubo-ovarian abscess
  5. Pelvic peritonitis
  6. Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome (perihepatitis — RUQ pain from peritoneal spread to liver capsule)

Classic presentation:

  • Lower abdominal/pelvic pain (bilateral, dull to severe)
  • Cervical motion tenderness ("chandelier sign" — so painful patient jumps off the table)
  • Adnexal tenderness
  • Uterine tenderness
  • Abnormal vaginal/cervical discharge (often purulent)
  • Dyspareunia
  • Abnormal uterine bleeding
  • Fever, chills (variable)

Long-term consequences (why prompt treatment matters!):

  • Infertility: Risk rises with each episode — 12% after 1 episode, 25% after 2, 50% after 3
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Risk increases 6-10x due to tubal scarring
  • Chronic pelvic pain (20-40%)
  • Tubo-ovarian abscess (surgical emergency if ruptures)
🎯 PID Treatment & Prevention

Outpatient (mild-moderate):

  • Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM × 1
  • Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID × 14 days
  • Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID × 14 days

Inpatient criteria: Pregnancy, severe illness, tubo-ovarian abscess, inability to tolerate oral meds, failed outpatient therapy

Critical teaching:

  • Treat ALL sexual partners from the past 60 days
  • No sex until both patient and partner(s) complete treatment
  • Complete ENTIRE antibiotic course even if feeling better
  • Retest 3 months after treatment
  • Use condoms consistently going forward
  • Follow up to confirm resolution

Prevention: Screen sexually active women <25 annually for chlamydia/gonorrhea; treat STIs promptly; condoms; limit partners.

🎯 Distinguishing by pH

Normal vaginal pH: 3.8-4.5

  • BV: pH >4.5, positive whiff test, clue cells
  • Yeast: normal pH, hyphae on KOH
  • Trichomoniasis: pH >4.5, motile trichomonads

Definition: No pregnancy after 12 months of regular unprotected intercourse (6 months if >35 years).

Workup
  • Semen Analysis — first test (male factor)
  • Ovulation confirmation — BBT, LH kits, mid-luteal progesterone
  • Tubal patency — Hysterosalpingogram (HSG)
  • Ovarian reserve — Day 3 FSH, AMH
