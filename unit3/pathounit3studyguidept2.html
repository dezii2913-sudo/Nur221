🎯 High-Yield Exam Focus

These are the MOST TESTED concepts from your instructor's PowerPoint. Master these first!

GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease) - HIGH YIELD

🔥 GERD Management

Pathophysiology: Lower esophageal sphincter allows stomach acid to reflux into esophagus

NCLEX Tips:

  • Wait 1 hour after eating before lying down
  • Eat 4-6 small meals instead of 3 large
  • Avoid trigger foods (spicy, fatty, caffeine, alcohol)
  • Elevate head of bed 6-8 inches
  • Sleep on LEFT side to reduce reflux

Cirrhosis - VERY HIGH YIELD

🫘 Cirrhosis & Lactulose

Why Lactulose? Cirrhosis impairs the liver's ability to convert ammonia to urea. Lactulose traps ammonia in the gut and eliminates it through stool.

NCLEX Alert: If a patient with cirrhosis says "I'm not constipated, I don't need lactulose" - educate that it's used to decrease AMMONIA levels, not just for constipation!

Signs of Advanced Cirrhosis:

  • Petechiae (low platelets)
  • Ascites
  • Jaundice
  • Hepatic encephalopathy
  • Portal hypertension

Bowel Obstruction - CRITICAL!

🚨 Small Bowel Obstruction

Withhold medications: Laxatives (Senna), stimulants - they can worsen obstruction!

Paralytic Ileus Prevention: Increase ambulation post-op to promote peristalsis

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) - HIGH YIELD

🫘 CKD Monitoring

Best measure of fluid retention: DAILY WEIGHT (most reliable!)

Lab Findings in AKI/CKD:

  • Elevated BUN
  • Elevated Creatinine
  • Hyperkalemia (dangerous!)
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Anemia (decreased erythropoietin)

🍽️ Chapter 17: Digestive System Disorders

📝 Teacher's Notes - Chapter 17

Digestion Pathway: Mouth → Esophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine → Large Intestine/Colon → Rectum; each portion has specific enzymes for breaking down substances
Neurovascular Control: Hypothalamus communicates with GI tract to signal when to eat and when enough nutrients are ingested
Nutrient Storage: Body stores nutrients in adipose (fat) tissue and liver for later use
Gluconeogenesis: Occurs in liver and kidneys after ~8 hours of fasting; supplies plasma glucose needs between meals
Water Survival: Adults survive ~10 days without water; children ~5 days
Mucous Membranes: Angular cheilitis (fissures in mouth corners) often indicates riboflavin (B2) deficiency; Purpura indicates vitamin K deficiency
Vomiting Reflex: 1) Deep breath, 2) Close glottis/raise soft palate, 3) Cease respiration (prevents aspiration), 4) Relax gastroesophageal sphincter, 5) Contract abdominal muscles, 6) Reverse peristaltic waves
Vomitus Colors: Hematemesis (coffee ground = bleeding), Yellow/green (bile from duodenum), Dark brown (lower intestine obstruction), Undigested food (gastric emptying problem)
GERD: CONCEPT MAP - Important disorder to know
Cirrhosis: CONCEPT MAP - Extensive fibrosis impairs metabolic processes
Intestinal Obstruction: CONCEPT MAP - Mechanical causes colicky pain/marked bowel sounds; paralytic ileus causes steady pain/absent bowel sounds
NCLEX GERD Teaching: Wait 3 hours after eating before bed; eat 4-6 small meals; elevate head of bed
Lactulose for Cirrhosis: Prevents ammonia absorption in colon; prevents hepatic encephalopathy
Cirrhosis Signs: Petechiae (impaired coagulation), hypotension, osteoporosis
SBO Medications: Withhold laxatives (Senna) - risk of perforation
Paralytic Ileus Prevention: Increase ambulation and fiber intake to promote peristalsis

Intake and Storage

Hunger and Satiety Regulation: Controlled by the hypothalamus based on feedback from the GI tract

  • Low blood glucose or empty GI tract → induces hunger
  • Food in GI tract → triggers stretch receptors and insulin secretion → reduces appetite
  • Fat stimulates cholecystokinin (CCK) → contributes to satiety
  • Increased fat stores → leptin release → suppresses appetite, increases metabolism

Storage Sites

  • Adipocytes: Store lipids as energy source
  • Liver: Stores vitamins A, B12, D, E, K, iron, copper, and glycogen

Digestion Process

StageLocationProcess
MechanicalMouth, StomachChewing, churning of food
ChemicalMouth → Stomach → Small IntestineEnzymes + bile convert substances into absorbable components

Digestion Pathway:

  1. Chewing → food mixes with saliva (salivary amylase initiates carb digestion)
  2. Stomach → mixes with HCl, pepsin (protein digestion initiated)
  3. Small intestine → bile + pancreatic enzymes (final digestion)

Normal Stool Characteristics

  • Firm, moist, semi-solid
  • Brown color (stercobilin - bilirubin pigment)
  • Transit time: 2-5 days from ingestion to elimination

Elimination Process

  1. Fecal matter enters cecum via ileocecal valve
  2. Propelled toward rectum via peristalsis
  3. Water and electrolytes removed in large intestine
  4. Parasympathetic stimulation → internal anal sphincter relaxation
  5. Pudendal nerve allows voluntary control of external sphincter

⭐ NCLEX Connection Questions (From PowerPoint)

GERD Question

A nurse is providing teaching for a client who has GERD. Which instruction should the nurse include?

  1. "Sleep on your left side."
  2. "Drink milk to soothe your stomach."
  3. "Eat four small meals each day."
  4. "Wait to go to bed for 1 hr after eating."

Answer: 4 - Wait at least 1 hour after eating before lying down to prevent reflux

Cirrhosis Question

A nurse is administering lactulose to a client with cirrhosis who states, "I don't need this medication. I am not constipated." The nurse should explain that lactulose is used to decrease levels of which component?

  1. Glucose
  2. Ammonia
  3. Potassium
  4. Bicarbonate

Answer: 2 - Lactulose decreases ammonia levels by trapping it in the gut for elimination

Bowel Obstruction Question

A nurse is reviewing medications for a client with a small bowel obstruction. Which medication should be withheld?

  1. Senna
  2. Ibuprofen
  3. Omeprazole
  4. Zolpidem

Answer: 1 - Senna is a laxative and should be withheld in bowel obstruction

🫘 Chapter 18: Urinary System Disorders

Nephrons
The functional units of the kidneys, forming urine through filtration, reabsorption, and secretion
Microscopic Hematuria
Small amounts of blood in urine; associated with infection, inflammation, or tumors
Gross Hematuria
Large numbers of RBCs visible in urine; indicates glomerular permeability or hemorrhage
Proteinuria/Albuminuria
Leakage of albumin or plasma proteins into filtrate; indicates inflammation and increased glomerular permeability
Bacteriuria
Bacteria in the urine; indicates UTI (pyuria = pus in urine)
Urinary Casts
Microscopic molds of tubules; indicates kidney tubule inflammation
Specific Gravity
Indicates tubules' ability to concentrate urine; very low = renal failure (assuming normal hydration)

Click to enlarge - Urinary system anatomy (kidneys, ureters, bladder)

Renal System Functions

  • Regulation of body fluid volume and composition
  • Elimination of metabolic wastes
  • Synthesis, release, or activation of hormones:
    • Erythropoietin: Stimulates RBC production
    • Renin: Blood pressure regulation
    • Vitamin D activation: Calcium metabolism
  • Regulation of blood pressure

Nephron Function

The kidneys process 20-25% of cardiac output (~1000 mL blood/minute)

  1. Filtration: Filter substances from blood
  2. Reabsorption: Return filtered nutrients and water to blood
  3. Secretion: Move waste into tubules for excretion

Normal Urine Characteristics

  • Color: Straw to yellow
  • Clarity: Clear
  • Odor: Slight ammonia smell
  • Volume: 750-2000 mL/day

Micturition Reflex

  1. Urine enters ureters → propelled by peristalsis to bladder
  2. Bladder filling activates stretch receptors
  3. Parasympathetic fibers stimulate detrusor muscle contraction
  4. Internal and external urethral sphincters relax
  5. Micturition (voiding) occurs

Altered Urinary Elimination - Causes

AlterationConsequences
MotilityReduced contraction → stasis, cast formation, obstruction risk
NeuromuscularFailure of neural signals → retention or incontinence
PerfusionGlobal (vasoconstriction, low volume) or Focal (embolism) → ischemia, pain
PatencyBlockage → dilation proximal to obstruction, stasis, infection

Clinical Manifestations

Altered volume (<750 mL or >2000 mL/day)
Cloudy or amber urine
Hematuria (blood)
Dysuria (painful urination)
Distention
Nausea, vomiting, anorexia
Fever (infection)

Types of Urinary Disorders

CategoryExamples
InfectionUTI (simple or complicated)

🫘 Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

Best Measure of Fluid Retention: DAILY WEIGHT (most reliable!)

Lab Findings:

  • ↑ BUN and Creatinine
  • ↑ Potassium (hyperkalemia) - dangerous!
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • ↓ Calcium, ↑ Phosphorus
  • Anemia (↓ erythropoietin)

🫘 Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

Lab Findings:

  • Elevated BUN
  • Hyperkalemia
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Fluid retention
🚨
Monitor for: Hyperkalemia can cause life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias!

⭐ NCLEX Connection Questions (From PowerPoint)

CKD Fluid Monitoring

A nurse is assessing a client who has chronic kidney disease for fluid volume increase. Which provides a reliable measure of fluid retention?

  1. Daily weight
  2. Sodium level
  3. Tissue turgor
  4. Intake and output

Answer: 1 - Daily weight is the most reliable measure of fluid retention

AKI Lab Findings

A nurse is monitoring a client who has acute kidney injury. Which laboratory finding should the nurse expect?

  1. Hypokalemia
  2. Metabolic alkalosis
  3. Hypercalcemia
  4. Elevated BUN

Answer: 4 - Elevated BUN is expected in AKI due to decreased kidney function

🧬 CORPUS LUTEUM - MUST KNOW!

The corpus luteum is formed from the ruptured ovarian follicle after ovulation. It is a temporary endocrine structure that:

  • Secretes large amounts of progesterone and estrogen
  • If pregnancy occurs: Maintains pregnancy until placenta takes over (~10-14 weeks)
  • If no pregnancy: Degenerates within ~14 days → hormone levels drop → menstruation

Follicular Phase

  1. FSH and LH stimulate secondary follicles
  2. Follicles enlarge and secrete estrogen
  3. Dominant follicle continues secreting estrogen
  4. Pituitary decreases FSH
  5. Oocyte breaks through follicle (ovulation)

Luteal Phase

  1. Ruptured follicle forms corpus luteum
  2. Corpus luteum secretes progesterone and estrogen
  3. If pregnancy: Progesterone supports pregnancy until placenta forms
  4. If no pregnancy: Corpus luteum degenerates → hormone drop → menstruation
